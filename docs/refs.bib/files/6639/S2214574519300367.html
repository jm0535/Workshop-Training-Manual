Volume 32, April 2019, Pages iii-v

Editorial overview: Communication in multitrophic interactions – new analytical approaches and emerging ecological consequences

Michael Singer is a professor in the Biology Department and the College of the Environment at Wesleyan University in Middletown, Connecticut, U.S.A. His research program focuses on ecology and evolution of multitrophic interactions, with an emphasis on host-plant use and dietary specialization of caterpillars. Some specific research projects include therapeutic medication by caterpillars in response to parasitoid infection, the critical role of herbivore diet breadth in mediating the cascading effects of bird predation on caterpillars and plants, and effects of landscape fragmentation on multitrophic networks.

Katerina Sam is an associate professor in the Entomology Institute of Biology Centre of CAS in Czech Republic. In 2013 she received her PhD studying bird communities along elevational gradients, their predation and interactions with insect. She is head of laboratory of Multitrophic Interactions, and her research has now expanded to various aspects of interactions between herbivores, plants, parasitoids, predators and abiotic factors.

Genoveva Rodríguez-Castañeda received her Ph.D. studying the role of multi-trophic interactions on the distribution of plants, herbivores, and insect predators. She expanded her research to include a macroecological and evolutionary approach to study biodiversity. She addresses how ecological interactions such as mutualisms, interspecific competition, and predation affect plant and insect diversity.
