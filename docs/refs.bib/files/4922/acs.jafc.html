    Abstract

    Coffee (Coffea arabica and C. canephora) is one of the most widely traded agricultural commodities and the main cash crop in âˆ¼80 tropical countries. Among the factors that limit coffee production, the coffee berry borer, Hypothenemus hampei (Ferrari) has been considered the main insect pest, causing losses of over U.S. $500 million dollars annually. Control of this pest has been hindered by two main factors: the cryptic nature of the insect (i.e., protected inside the coffee berry) and the availability of coffee berries in the field allowing the survival of the pest from one generation to the next. Coffee berry borer control has primarily been based on the use of synthetic insecticides. Management strategies have focused on the use of African parasitoids (Cephalonomia stephanoderis, Prorops nasuta, and Phymastichus coffea), fungal entomopathogens (Beauveria bassiana), and insect traps. These approaches have had mixed results. Recent work on the basic biology of the insect has provided novel insights that might be useful in developing novel pest management strategies. For example, the discovery of symbiotic bacteria responsible for caffeine breakdown as part of the coffee berry borer microbiome opens new possibilities for pest management via the disruption of these bacteria. Some chemicals with repellent propieties have been identified, and these have a high potential for field implementation. Finally, the publication of the CBB genome has provided insights on the biology of the insect that will help us to understand why it has been so successful at exploiting the coffee plant. Here I discuss the tools we now have against the CBB and likely control strategies that may be useful in the near future.

    Coffee (Coffea sp.) is predominantly an African genus that comprises 124 species.(1,2) Arabica coffee (Coffea arabica L.) and Robusta coffee (Coffea canephora Pierre ex A. Froehner) are the two commercial species that are widely cultivated and used in the production of coffee. Although Africa is the origin of both species, they came from different environments; C. arabica originated in the upland evergreen forests of southern Ethiopia, whereas C. canephora is native to the lowland humid forests between Uganda and Cameroon.(1,3) Both species have been introduced into many tropical and subtropical countries, where they are now among the most important cash crops. Approximately 11 million hectares worldwide are planted with coffee producing approximately 9 million tonnes annually.(4) Despite the fluctuating prices of the world market, coffee remains one of the most valuable agricultural commodity in international world trade. The economic revenue in coffee producing countries is about U.S. $12 billion annually, while the value of the coffee industry has been estimated at U.S. $173 billion.(5)
    The perennial evergreen nature of coffee favors the attraction of a number of arthropods.(6) More than 3000 species of insects and mites have been associated with coffee, either as phytophagous arthropods or their predators and parasitoids.(7) Of these, more than 850 species of insect are known to feed on the coffee tree,(8) and approximately 30 species cause economic losses, including the coffee berry borer (Curculionidae), leaf miners (Lyonetiidae), antestia bugs (Pentatomidae), stem borers (Cerambycidae), twig borers (Curculionidae), whiteflies (Aleyrodidae), scales (Coccidae), aphids (Aphididae), and mealybugs (Pseudococcidae).(6,9,10)
    With losses over U.S. $500 million annually,(11) the coffee berry borer, Hypothenemus hampei (Ferrari), is the most devastating pest of coffee worldwide.(5,9) Originally from Central Africa, this pest has now been reported in almost every country where the coffee plant has been introduced. The first report on the presence of H. hampei outside Africa came from Indonesia in 1908.(12) Later, this pest invaded the Americas through Brazil in 1913.(13,14) From Brazil, H. hampei dispersed to other coffee growing areas in the Americas and the Caribbean. Studies involving molecular methods to track the dissemination of the CBB suggested that there were three separate introductions to the Americas and that West Africa was the origin of introductions into America and Asia.(15) After this, the CBB slowly spread to the rest of coffee producing countries in the world. The most recent detections of this insect in Puerto Rico in 2007,(16) Hawaii in 2010,(17) and Papua New Guinea in 2016, show that phytosanitary measures to stop H. hampei had failed in every country.
    Because H. hampei feeds and reproduces within the coffee seeds inside the coffee berry, it is considered a direct pest that negatively affects the crop by causing losses in yield and quality. Green and ripe berries are susceptible to attack by the insect.(9) The dry matter content of the endosperm is the critical factor determining attack; green berries with less than 20% dry matter in the endosperm are either abandoned after an initial attack or the female waits for several days in the tunnel she has bored until the endosperm has developed.(18,19) Although the coffee berry borer does not breed in green fruit with less than 20% dry matter, these berries are often lost, either by premature fall or by decay, because H. hampei damage allows the entry of saprophytic microorganisms.(18,20,21) Severe damage also occurs when fruit have more that 20% of dry matter (approximately 2â€“3 months after flowering), because at this stage adult females colonize the seeds (endosperm).(22) During their life cycle, adults and larvae of H. hampei consume the endosperm, thereby greatly reducing the quality and economic value of infested seeds, which can only be sold as a low-grade product.(20) More than 100 individuals (eggs, larvae, and adults) have been recorded in a single coffee fruit,(23) and an estimated density of 11 million borers per hectare has been reported in Mexican coffee plantations.(24) Such high infestation levels are difficult to manage at this stage, and pest management measures should be performed at earlier stages of infestation, as should become clear farther on. As several reviews have been published on the biology and ecology of this insect,(5,19âˆ’21,25) the aim of the present work is to propose a reasonable strategy to manage the coffee berry borer infestations.

    Several pest management strategies have been used against the coffee berry borer. One of the oldest methods is the cultural (manual) control, i.e., the removal and destruction of infested coffee berries (which serve as source for new infestations) to reduce the population levels. In theory, this is perhaps the most effective method of control against the insect that may perform at any stage of coffee fruit development.(26âˆ’28) In Colombia, the manual removal of mature berries was found to eliminate approximately 80% of the population.(27,29) Despite being effective and environmentally advantageous, this practice greatly increases the costs of production.(30)
    Synthetic insecticides have been widely used against the coffee berry borer. The commercial availability, ease of application in the field, and insecticidal efficacy have favored their use in some countries. Brazil pioneered the use of synthetic organic insecticides against H. hampei in 1947,(31) and this practice was adopted extensively by African countries in 1949.(32) In the 1960s, the broad-spectrum organochlorine insecticide endosulfan became the most effective and widely used synthetic compound for controlling the insect. A single application of endosulfan reduced CBB infestation up to 88% and provided good control for up to 12 weeks.(32) Extensive use of this insecticide resulted in development of resistance in New Caledonia,(33) and in other problems like chronic human intoxication, toxicity to aquatic fauna and nontarget organisms, and environmental persistence of up to eight months.(34) For these reasons, endosulfan has been banned in at least 70 countries.(35,36) Other insecticides have been evaluated against H. hampei with promising results. For instance, pirimiphos-methyl, fenitrothion, chlorpyrifos, and fenthion resulted in 98% pest mortality when applied at the time the CBB was boring into the coffee berry.(37)
    Biological control of H. hampei using three African parasitoids has been another method widely used in many countries since the 1980s. The parasitoids Prorops nasuta Waterston (Bethylidae), Cephalonomia stephanoderis Betrem (Bethylidae), and Phymastichus coffea LaSalle (Eulophidae) have been introduced in over 15 countries outside Africa. This pest management strategy is known as classical biological control and unfortunately results with the coffee berry borer have not been satisfactory.(14) All three species have been unable to maintain high population levels in the field, such that multiple releases of parasitoids have to be performed through the growing season.(5) Although the establishment of these species occurs in most coffee plantations, parasitoid populations decreased dramatically in the absence of frequent releases and the pest population does not fall below the economic threshold.(5,21,27,29,39) The effect of parasitoids on the coffee berry borer has been unsatisfactory as a single method of control and other measures of control are needed to check the pest.(29,38,39) In the case of biological control using entomopathogens, the cosmopolitan fungus Beauveria bassiana (Balsamo) Vuillemin (Ascomicota: Hypocreales) has been found infecting H. hampei adults in coffee plantations wherever the borer is present. The incidence of B. bassiana is usually higher when there are young berries attacked by the insect and under rainy conditions.(40) The fungus has been widely used throughout the world in attempts to control the coffee berry borer. A common practice to increase the natural infection of B. bassiana is to culture isolates collected from the field and spray the conidial suspensions on coffee berry borer infested fruit.(41) This practice can result in levels of mortality up to 84% under field conditions,(42) similar to the performance of synthetic insecticides. However, the main disadvantages of using B. bassiana is the slow infection process that allows the adults live long enough to damage the coffee berry, the fast deactivation of conidia after spraying, and high production costs.(30,40,43)
    Several types of traps to capture coffee berry borer adults have been developed. One of these traps is commercially available under the name BROCAP and uses a mixture of ethanol and methanol as an attractant and has been employed in numerous countries. This trap can be used permanently throughout the coffee producing cycle but is likely to be more valuable in capturing residual adults after the harvest period. According to Dufour,(44) a single BROCAP trap can capture more than 10â€¯000 H. hampei adults per day when infestations are high. However, to reduce costs, most coffee growers use artisanal traps to capture this insect. In Mexico, weekly captures of artisanal traps ranged from 83 to 1484,(45) whereas in Brazil, other traps have captured 77 to 609 adults per week.(46) However, effective in capturing coffee berry borer adults, the use of these traps a single method of control do not solve the problem, and other measures of control are needed. It is important to point out that alcohol-based traps are not specific to the CBB and they may capture and kill many other insects not considered as pests.(47)

    An effective strategy to control the coffee berry borer should involve an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) approach, in which multiple tactics are combined to reduce the pest populations to tolerable levels while maintaining quality environment.(48) By using the available pest control technologies against the coffee berry borer, control measures should start when coffee is being harvested (Figure 1). In fact, the harvesting and processing of coffee is itself a major mortality factor for the H. hampei population, where most individuals inside the berry die, while a few others escape from the coffee fermentation tanks and return to the field.(49âˆ’51) Considering that the coffee berry borer can only feed and reproduce in coffee, it is important to reduce the number of surviving adults that will eventually infest the fruit of the following coffee cycle. In countries that have only one coffee harvest per year, the recommendations are (i) to carry out efficient harvesting, avoiding leaving residual fruit on coffee trees or on the ground, and (ii) to use coffee berry borer traps after harvesting to catch and kill residual adults. The effectiveness of these two measures will determine the prevalence of the CBB infestation in the next cycle. The use of traps may continue when fruit are absent in the field and until the following fruiting cycle. A density of 22 traps per hectare uniformly distributed, drastically decreased the prevalence of CBB infestation from one season to the next.(52)
    Coffee flowering begins immediately after the rainy season.(53) Depending on the frequency of rainfall, there may be one, two, or more flowering periods. As a consequence, several fructifications may occur in a single year, but there will be always a main fructification that will concentrate most of the coffee berries.(54) Depending on the altitude of coffee plantations, berries will be susceptible to the H. hampei attack 2â€“3 months after flowering.(18,22) Coffee berry borer adults can survive up to 156 days in fruit left in the field on trees or on the ground after harvesting.(9) The surviving adults will subsequently leave these fruits to infest the new ones. It is at this time that sprays of B. bassiana are recommended, as conditions at this moment are usually ideal for effective pest control: low population levels of the pest, high humidity favoring survival and germination of the fungus, starving and possibly weak adults that may be more prone to fungal infection, and individuals outside the berry or just initiating the boring of the berry, among others. A novel trap that combines the use of alcohols and B. bassiana has been recently developed.(55) Using a mixture of ethanol and methanol, the trap attracts colonizing females, contaminate them with B. bassiana, and disperse the fungus in coffee plantations after CBB females exited the device. With a mortality of CBB adult females from 66 to 92% under field conditions, this trap has a great potential to be used as an IPM component.(55) Releases of the coffee berry borer adult parasitoid Phymastichus coffea can be undertaken 2â€“3 weeks after B. bassiana sprays in order to reduce the risk of parasitoid mortality due to fungal infection.(56) The higher the number of H. hampei adults controlled by these two natural enemies, the smaller the number of progeny and coffee berry damage at harvest.
    Coffee berry borer adults that are not killed by B. bassiana or P. coffea, will penetrate and colonize coffee fruits. Inside the seed, the CBB female will oviposit and larvae will feed on the same seed to grow and reach the adult stage.(9) The generation time in the field is âˆ¼45 days.(57) Mortality of the coffee berry borer population during this phase can be increased through releases of the parasitoids C. stephanoderis and P. nasuta, which parasitizes larvae and pupae of the CBB.(20)
    Although all the elements for this conceptual strategy have been known for a long time and are implemented in some coffee producing countries, their systematic use to achieve a sustainable coffee berry borer management has not been undertaken for several reasons. In some cases only one or two types of control are used regardless the fruiting phenology of coffee. In other cases, control measures against H. hampei are initiated too late in the season, when the insect is already inside the berry and has reached high levels of infestation. Under this situation control is difficult to achieve because the insect is protected within the fruit and has already reproduced. For this reason it is necessary to combine the above-mentioned methods of control with due attention to timing and fruiting phenology, in order to obtain adequate control of this pest.

    Studies on the microbiota of the coffee berry borer have revealed a wide array of microorganisms associated with this insect. PÃ©rez et al.(58) reported 39 species of fungi in 21 genera isolated from the insect cuticle, gut, and feces. Fusarium, Penicillium, Candida, and Aspergillus were the dominant genera. CarriÃ³n and Bonet(59) identified 12 fungal species associated with the adult stage. More recently, Ceja-Navarro et al.(60) identified 13 bacterial species in the alimentary canal that were able to breakdown caffeine: Brachybacterium rhamnosum, Enterobacter sp., Jonesiaceae, Kosakonia cowanii, Microbacterium binotii, Novosphigobium sp., Ochrobactrum sp., Pantoea vagans, P. septica, P. eucalypti, Pseudomonas fulva, P. fluorescens, and Stenotrophomonas maltophilia. Antibiotics added to the H. hampei artificial diet eliminated caffeine degradation, demonstrating the involvement of bacteria in the process. The caffeine demethylase gene (ndmA) was expressed in vivo in field specimens as well as in P. fulva isolated from the coffee berry borer gut. Diet inoculation with P. fulva restored the ability to degrade caffeine (Figure 2). The remarkable discovery of caffeine detoxification in H. hampei opens new research options to manage this pest. Right now is too early to propose a practical way to use this information, but the basic idea would be to find a mechanism to interfere with the bacteria involved in caffeine detoxification. This eventual interference would likely result in the death of the insect.
    Very little is known about the genome of coleopteran species, and a recent study reported the genome of H. hampei, the third species reported for this order of insects.(61) The coffee berry borer genome is approximately 163 Mb with 19222 predicted protein-coding genes. Genome analysis revealed four important aspects of the CBB biology: (i) indications of 10 cases of putative horizontal gene transfers from bacteria, (ii) paralogous expansion of the antimicrobial peptide repertoire, (iii) the presence of enzymes involved in the degradation of complex polysaccharides, and (iv) the presence of the gene Rdl that confers resistance to cyclodiene insecticides, such as endosulfan.(60) However, more research is needed, the study provides valuable information that may prove useful in the development of novel technologies for the management of this insect.
    The identification and use of repellents is another promising tool against the CBB. Different degrees of repellency of verbenone and methylcyclohexenone were reported in the coffee berry borer.(62) Jaramillo et al.(63) confirmed H. hampei avoidance to verbenone and mentioned that Î±-pinene was another repellent for this insect. Vega et al.(64) identified a sesquiterpene, (E,E)-Î±-farnesene, as a potential repellent of the coffee berry borer produced by infested coffee fruits, with remarkable results in a field test in Hawaii. The results showed up to an 80% decrease in CBB captures in traps with 3:1 methanolâ€“ethanol attractant, and a bubble cap formulation of (E,E)-Î±-farnesene, compared to traps containing the attractant only.
    To conclude, it is clear that the current technologies for coffee berry borer control require intensive labor and careful monitoring to keep the pest under control. Recent studies on the microbiota of H. hampei, its genome, and chemical repellents have greatly expanded our knowledge of this insect and will likely provide novel insights of use in the development of pest managemernt strategies in the near future.
