Abstract

A survey of Lepidoptera reared from seeds and fruits primarily on Barro Colorado Island, Panama, yielded 444 specimens of Tortricidae representing 20 species. Nearly 90% of reared tortricids and 65% of the species are members of the tribe Grapholitini, a group that includes numerous economically important pests of fruit worldwide. We present host records for the following species: Histura panamana Brown, Platynota obliqua Walsingham complex, Platynota subargentea Walsingham, Spinipogon triangularis Brown, Cryptaspasma perseana Gilligan and Brown, Steblopotamia streblopa (Meyrick), Cydia pyraspis (Meyrick) complex, Eriosocia guttifera (Meyrick), Riculorampha ancyloides Rota and Brown, Grapholita mabea Razowski, Ricula croceus Brown, Ricula lacistema Brown, Ricula sp. 1, Ricula sp. 2, Talponia sp. 1, Talponia sp. 2, and four unidentified Grapholitini. In a comparison of studies focused on fruit- and seed-feeding insects in Thailand, Panama, and Kenya, Grapholitini represented 73% (in Thailand) to 90% (in Panama) of the total number of reared specimens of Tortricidae, and 45% (in Kenya) to 65% (in Panama) of the total number of tortricid species. However, a similar survey in Papua New Guinea produced considerably different results, with Grapholitini representing 46% of the tortricid specimens and only 20% of the species.

Tortricidae are among the most economically important Lepidoptera pests of ornamental, forest, and agricultural plants (Zhang 1994), yet larval hosts are unknown for the vast majority (ca. 85%) of the more than 11,000 described species (Brown et al. 2010). Host data are particularly uncommon for tortricids of the Neotropical Region, where the greatest species richness of the family occurs. Scattered, mostly single-species or single host contributions on the subject for the Neotropical region include papers by Pastrana (1950, 1952, 1961), Biezanko et al. (1974), Pereyra and Sanchez (1998), Brown and Passoa (1998), White (1999), Diniz et al. (2001), Brown (2004, 2008), de Armas and Nunez (2005), Razowski and Brown (2008, 2018), Brown et al. (2011, 2019), Cepeda and Cubillos (2011), Cabrera-Asencio et al. (2013), Rocca and Brown (2013), Vargas (2013), Bobadilla (2015), and Janzen and Hallwachs (2018). Most of the host records included in these papers are incorporated into the on-line databases of Robinson et al. (2006) and Brown et al. (2010).

A project focused on rearing insects from seeds and fruits in Panama from 2010 to 2013 yielded 444 specimens of Tortricidae from the fruit of 28 different species of Neotropical plants. Most of the host plant records are new. The purpose of this paper is to present the host data for the tortricid species reared during the project. A few additional host records were obtained from specimens deposited in the National Museum of Natural History, Washington, D.C., U.S.A. (USNM) and the Museo Nacional de Costa Rica, San Jose Costa Rica (MNCR).

Materials and Methods

Study Site. Barro Colorado Island (BCI) (centroid ca. 9.1521, -79.8465) is located in the man-made Gatun Lake in the middle of the Panama Canal. The island was formed in 1913 when the Chagres River was dammed to form the lake. When the waters rose, they covered a significant part of the existing tropical forest, but certain hilltops remained as islands in the middle of the lake. BCI has an area of 15.6 km2.

Survey Methods. Between July 2010 and November 2013 seeds and fruits were collected in Barro Colorado Nature Monument, primarily on BCI, but occasionally on surrounding mainland peninsulas with a similar forest composition. On a weekly basis, sections of the trail network of BCI were walked, and freshly fallen seeds and fruits (including immature fruits) of trees, shrubs, and lianas were collected with the goal of obtaining a representative sample of species fruiting at any given time. In addition to opportunistic seed and fruit collecting along trails, monthly lists of target plant species known to produce seeds and fruits during a specific month of the year (e.g., Wright et al. 2005, Wright and Calderon 2006) were distributed to a team of experienced botanical field technicians who searched for fruit of these species. To avoid potential contamination of fruit by scavengers and/or secondary invaders, only freshly fallen, intact-looking seeds and fruits were collected. Where possible, seeds and fruits still attached to the plant were collected, mostly from branches that could be reached by hand or using a telescopic pole pruner. A small subset of the seed and fruit samples were obtained from a network of seed fall traps in the 50-ha “forest dynamics” plot (Wright et al. 2005).

Rearing Methods. All seeds and fruit were brought to the laboratory, sorted according to species and degree of maturity (mature versus immature), counted, and stored in plastic pots lined with tissue paper and covered with a lid of fine mesh. Samples collected on different days were stored in separate pots. All plant samples were identified to species. The pots were stored on shelves in a shaded house under conditions resembling ambient forest understory conditions for a period of three months. During the rearing period, each pot was checked approximately twice a week to harvest adult insects. Specimens of Lepidoptera were pinned, spread, and dried. After three months the seeds were dissected to remove any remaining insects that had not emerged.

Identification. All adult insects emerging from seeds or soil in pots were assigned to a morphospecies. Specimens reared from different plant species initially were processed separately and given different morphospecies-by-plant species codes. These were later merged based on examination of selected specimens by taxonomic experts. To assist in identifications and ensure that cryptic species (e.g., Hebert et al. 2004) were not present, several specimens (up to 10) per morphospecies were DNA barcoded. Tissue samples (one leg of an adult moth) were used to amplify a ∼650bp region of the mitochondrial gene cytochrome oxidase 1 (COI - barcode) using standard procedures at the Biodiversity Institute of Ontario, University of Guelph (Craft et al. 2010, Wilson 2012) with the primers LepF1, MLepF1, LepR1, MLepR2 (Hajibabaei et al. 2006), LCO-1490, and HCO-2198 (Folmer et al. 1994). Pairwise Kimura 2-parameter (K2P) distances (Kimura 1980) were calculated among sequences and used to construct a neighbor-joining tree. We provide the BOLD Barcode Index Number (BIN) for each species listed below. The BIN system clusters barcode sequences algorithmically to a level wherein the resulting clusters show high concordance with species; hence, BINs usually can be used to verify species identifications (Hajibabaei et al. 2006).

Plant names. Plant names (Table 1) follow the nomenclature available on the online “Flora of Barro Colorado Island” (STRI 2019).

Deposition. Specimens will be deposited in the collections of the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institution, Barro Colorado Island, Panama; and the National Museum of Natural History, Washington, D.C., U.S.A.

Results

A total of 444 specimens of tortricid moths representing 20 species was recorded from the study. Most of the host-moth relationships are new; a few were reported with the descriptions of four new species based on the reared material (i.e., Brown 2019b). Several of the moth species are undescribed but some of these can be associated with described genera through barcodes and/or genitalia; and these records provide insight into tortricid genera host preferences and breadth. The data are summarized by plant family in Table 1. Images of representative examples of the reared tortricids are presented in Figs. 1–8.

Chlidanotinae: Polyorthini Histura panamana Brown, 2019
[BIN: ABV2176]

  • The only species of Histura for which the larval host has been reported previously is H. perseavora Brown, 2010, which is a fruit and stem borer in Persea americana (Lauracaeae) in Guatemala (Brown and Hoddle 2010, Hoddle and Brown 2011).

  • During the study in Panama we recorded Histura panamana (n = 22) (Fig. 1) only on Beilschmiedia pendula (Lauraceae). Four undescribed species of Histura near berylla Razowski and Becker, 2011 have been reared in Costa Rica by Janzen and Hallwachs (2018) on Ocotea austinii, O. puberula, O. atirrensis, O. insularis, O. cernua, Nectandra hihua, and Licaria sp. (all Lauraceae). However, these records are most likely of larvae feeding on leaves rather than fruit. The related Histurodes costaricana Razowski, 1984 has been reared on leaves of Persea americana (Razowski 1984, USNM, MNCR). The combined data suggest that Histura and the related Histurodes may be specialists on Lauraceae, on leaves as well as fruit.

    Tortricinae: Sparganothini Platynota obliqua Walsingham, 1913
    complex [BIN: ABV2162]

  • Platynota obliqua includes three BINS [ABV2162, ABY5144, AAF1036] of specimens that are morphologically indistinguishable. Because it is impossible to determine which BIN represents “true” P. obliqua, we refer to all specimens as Platynota obliqua complex.

  • Nearly all species of Platynota are polyphagous leaf-rollers that are encountered uncommonly on fruit (Powell and Brown 2012). There are no previously published host records for P. obliqua, but the database of Janzen and Hallwachs (2018) includes records of ABY5144 on Conostegia xalapensis (Melastomataceae) (n = 3), Inga mortoniana (Fabaceae), Mimosa quadrivalvis (Fabaceae) (n = 1), Serjania schiedeana (Sapindaceae) (n = 1), and Acalypha diversifolia (Euphorbiaceae) (n = 1). The same database also includes records of AAF1036 on Hyptis obtusifolia (Lamiaceae) (n = 1), Inga edulis (Fabaceae) (n = 1), Inga oerstediana (Fabaceae) (n = 1), Machaerium seemannii (Fabaceae) (n = 1), Gouania polygama (Rhamnaceae) (n = 1), Nectandra hihua (Lauraceae) (n = 1), Zygia longifolia (Fabaceae) (n = 1), Mikania cordifolia (Asteraceae) (n = 1), Pouteria reticulata (Sapotaceae) (n = 1), and Zanthoxylum riedelianum (Rutaceae) (n = 1).

  • In Panama we reared P. obliqua [ABV2162] from fruits of Paullinia turbacensis (Sapindaceae) (n = 1), Heisteria concinna (Olacaceae) (n = 1), and Pseudobombax septenatum (Malvaceae) (n = 1). These host records support previous observations that most Platynota are polyphagous and may occasionally attack fruit as well as leaves of the host.

    • Platynota subargentea Walsingham, 1913 [BIN: AAA0948]

  • Although currently unresolved, it is likely that Platynota subargentea is a senior synonym of P. helianthes (Meyrick, 1932), which has been recorded from Casearia corymbosa (Flacortiaceae), Jatropha gossypifolia (Euphorbiaceae), Leucaena leucocephala (Fabaceae), Mimosa pigra (Fabaceae), Parkinsonia aculeata (Fabaceae), and Psidium guajava (Myrtaceae) (Brown et al. 2011). Janzen and Hallwachs (2018) also report dozens of hosts for this species from Costa Rica, spanning many plant families.

  • We reared a single specimen of Platynota subargentea from Zuelania guidonia (Salicaceae) on Barro Colorado Island.

    • Tortricinae: Euliini: Cochylina
    Spinipogon rectangularis Brown,
    2019 [BIN: AAA2890]

  • As recently redefined (Brown 2019a), the genus Spinipogon includes 11 species distributed from Ohio, U.S.A., to Santa Catarina, Brazil. Life histories and larval food plants are unknown for the genus.

  • We reared nine specimens of Spinipogon rectangularis (Fig. 2), eight from Guapira standleyana (Nyctaginaceae), and one from Tabebuia guayacan (Bignoniaceae). Although many Cochylina exhibit a preference for Asteraceae, there are many deviations from this pattern, and Spinipogon appears to represent another example of this.

    • Olethreutinae: Microcorsini
    Cryptaspasma perseana Gilligan &
    Brown, 2011 [BIN: AAB0277]

  • The few species of Cryptaspasma for which larval hosts have been recorded feed on the hard kernels or seeds of a variety of different plant families (Brown and Brown 2004, Dugdale et al. 2005, Gilligan et al. 2011), suggesting that plant architecture rather than plant chemistry determines host choice. At least two species have apparently expanded their dietary breadth from native hosts to cultivated plants and are now considered pests: C. querula (Meyrick, 1912) on Eugenia species (Myrtaceae) and stone fruits (Rosaceae) in New Zealand (Dugdale et al. 2005) and C. perseana Gilligan and Brown, 2011 on avocado (Persea americana) (Lauraceae) in Central America (Gilligan et al. 2011).

  • We reared C. perseana (n = 6) from the seeds of Prioria copaifera (Fabaceae). This new host record is consistent with our knowledge of hosts for the genus, i.e., larvae prefer seeds to leaves and appear to lack host plant family fidelity.

    • Olethreutinae: Olethreutini
    Streblopotamia streblopa (Meyrick, 1936) [BIN: ABV2183]

  • Streblopotamia streblopa was described from Guatemala by Meyrick (1936), who referred to two specimens from Panama (deposited in the USNM) reared from the fruit of Protium panamense (Burseraceae). While this plant species is known from Costa Rica and Panama (Mitré 1998), we have examined specimens of S. streblopa (Fig. 3) from a much broader geographic range, including the Dominican Republic, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, and Venezuela (Razowski and Brown 2018). Hence, other plants must serve as larval hosts.

  • During this study we reared a single specimen of S. streblopa from Beilschmiedia pendula (Lauraceae), a plant species known from Costa Rica, Panama, and the West Indies (USDA 2006).

    • Olethreutinae: Grapholitini
    Cydia pyraspis (Meyrick, 1928)
    complex [BIN: AAA4047, ACJ4819]

  • Specimens identified as Cydia pyraspis fall into three BINS (AAA4047, ACJ4819, AAA4020), suggesting the presence of cryptic species, one of which may be C. rhodaspis (Meyrick, 1928). This complex belongs to a group of closely related species that includes C. ninana (Riley, 1891) from southwestern U.S.A. and Mexico; C. rhodaspis from Costa Rica to Peru; C. sagittula Razowski, 2011 from Mexico; and one or more undescribed species from Central America. Cydia pyraspis complex has been reared from Psidium guajava (Myrtaceae) in Trinidad and Tobago; C. rhodaspis from Inga vera (Fabaceae) in Costa Rica; and C. sagittula Razowski from Inga sp. (Fabaceae) in Mexico.

  • We reared C. pyraspis complex (Fig. 4) from Inga umbellifera [AAA4047] (n = 1) and I. multijuga [ACJ4819] (n = 1) (both Fabaceae). Although our two specimens are morphologically indistinguishable, their barcodes are 3.1% different, indicating they are likely two different species. Regardless, these records are highly consistent with previous rearings of members of this species group from Central America; however, the record of Psidium from Trinidad and Tobago seems unusual.

    • Eriosocia guttifera (Meyrick, 1913)
    [BIN: ABV2165]

  • Laspeyresia guttifera (Fig. 5) was described by Meyrick (1913) from a single male from Paraguay. Razowski and Brown (2008) proposed the monotypic genus Eriosocia for the species and provided data on its morphology, geographic distribution, and hosts. They indicate that larvae have been reared on numerous occasions in Colombia and Panama from the fruit of Garcinia madruno (Clusiaceae).

  • We reared this species from Garcinia intermedia (Clusiaceae) (n = 3) and Symphonia globulifera (Clusiaceae) (n = 97) in Panama. These new records are consistent with the observations of Razowski and Brown (2008) and suggest this species is a specialist on Clusiaceae.

    • Riculorampha ancyloides Rota and Brown, 2009 [BIN: ABV2167]

  • Rota and Brown (2009) described this monotypic genus (Fig. 8) from specimens reared on Persea borbonia (Lauraceae) in Florida. In Panama we reared it from the fruit of Ocotea whitei (n = 2), Ocotea oblonga (n = 7), Nectandra cissiflora (n = 17), Nectandra lineata (n = 4), Nectandra globosa (n = 5), and Cinnamomum triplinerve (n = 4) (all Lauraceae). Host records suggest that this species is restricted to Lauraceae.

    • Grapholita mabeae Razowski, 2011
    [BIN: ABV2185]

  • Grapholita mabea was described from a single female reared from Mabea occidentalis (Euphorbiaceae) on Barro Colorado Island, Panama (Razowski 2011) (USNM). This species is almost certainly the same as “Laspeyresia sp.” of De Steven (1981), who described predation density and damage caused by this species to the fruit of Mabea occidentalis on Barro Colorado Island.

  • We also reared this species from Mabea occidentalis, and with 81 specimens, it was among the most frequently collected tortricids during the survey. According to Govaerts et al. (2000), Mabea occidentalis ranges from central Mexico to central Brazil.

  • The genus Grapholita is worldwide in distribution (Komai 1999), and members of the genus utilize a wide range of plant families as larval hosts. Nonetheless, related congeners frequently feed on plants in the same genus or family, with most species feeding on Fabaceae and Rosaceae. Grapholita mabeae is the only Grapholita known to feed on Euphorbiaceae, although a few species have been recorded from the related Sapindaceae.

    • Ricula spp. [BINs: ABV2186, AAY4670, ACG2639, ACJ4292]

  • Larval host plants are known for only two species of Ricula: Ricula gallicola (Pastrana, 1952) on Iodina rhombifolia (Santalaceae) (Pastrana 1952) and Ricula maculana (Fernald, 1901) on Schoepfia schreberi (Olacaceae) (Kimball 1965).

  • Among the tortricids reared from fruit in Panama are four species of Ricula: R. croceus Brown, 2019 [ABV2186], R. lacistema Brown, 2019 [AAY4670], and two undescribed species [ACG2639, ACJ4292]. Ricula croceus (Fig. 6) was reared from Heisteria concinna (Olacaceae) (n = 81) and H. acuminata (Olacaceae) (n = 16). Ricula lacistema (Fig. 7) was reared from Lacistema aggregatum (Lacistemataceae) (n = 21). Ricula sp. 1 [ACG2639] was reared from Trichilia tuberculata (Meliaceae) (n = 1), and Ricula sp. 2 [ACJ4292] from Casearia commersoniana (Salicaceae) (n = 4).

  • Although Olacaceae is used by two of the species of Ricula for which larval hosts are known, other species deviate from this pattern, using Santalaceae, Lacistemataceae, and Salicaceae. This lack of host family fidelity at the generic level is similar to that suggested below for the closely related Talponia.

    • Talponia spp. [BINs: ABV2175, ACG2213]

  • The genus Talponia ranges from the eastern U.S.A. to South America. Two species have been reared: Talponia batesi Heinrich, 1932 from the fruit of Annona cheromoya, A. glabra, and A. muricata (Annonaceae); and Talponia plummeriana (Busck, 1906) from the developing ovaries of Asimina triloba (Annonaceae).

  • We reared two species of Talponia from fruit in Panama: Talponia sp. 1 [ABV2175] from Desmopsis panamensis (Annonaceae) (n = 44) and Omphalea diandra (Euphorbiaceae) (n = 1); and Talponia sp. 2 [ACG2213] from Macrocnemum glabrescens (Rubiaceae) (n = 11). These host families deviate from the pattern observed elsewhere in which most species and/or closely related congeners in Grapholitini are restricted to a single plant genus or at least family. Here we observe Annonaceae, Euphorbiaceae, and Rubiaceae all as host plants for the genus. Similar patterns are exhibited by other large genera (e.g., Cydia, Grapholita) in which species groups appear to show host fidelity, at least at the plant-family level. Perhaps a species group (e.g., batesi, plummeriana, sp. 1) within the genus is restricted to Annonaceae.

    • Grapholitini spp. [BINs: ACG1391, ACJ4358, ABV2351]

  • Four additional species of Grapholitini defied confident assignment to genus: Dichrorampha? sp. [ACG1391], Grapholita? sp. [ACJ4358], Cydia? sp. [ABV2351], and unknown sp. [no barcode]. The suspected species of Dichrorampha [ACG1391] was reared from Picramnia latifolia (Picramniaceae) (n = 6); the possible Grapholita species from Hasseltia floribunda (Salicaceae) (n = 1); the possible Cydia species from Platypodium elegans (Fabaceae) (n = 1); and the unknown species from Doliocarpus olivaceus (Dilleniaceae) (n = 5).

    • Discussion

    Whereas most tortricid leaf rollers (e.g., members of the tribes Archipini, Sparganothini, and Atteriini) are moderately to highly polyphagous, most tortricid fruit and seed feeders exhibit a much greater degree of host fidelity (Regier et al. 2012). At one extreme is Grapholitini, a tribe that includes many host plant specialists that are restricted to a single plant species, genus, or family. In the study in Panama, approximately 90% of the tortricid specimens and 65% of the tortricid species could be assigned to Grapholitini (Table 2). Hence, it can be concluded that among Tortricidae, Grapholitini are by far the predominant fruit and seed feeders in this Neotropical community.

    Table 2.

    Comparison of number of Tortricidae specimens and species reared from fruit in Panama, Thailand, Kenya, and New Guinea with reference to Grapholitini.

    A similar study conducted on fruit- and seed-feeding insects in Thailand (Bassett et al. 2019) yielded 337 specimens of Tortricidae representing 16 species, of which 242 specimens (73%) and eight species (50%) were Grapholitini (Table 2) (Brown et al. 2019), a pattern similar to that found in Panama.

    In a study of tortricids reared from native fruit in Kenya, Brown et al. (2014) recorded approximately 103 species (or species complexes) from fruit, nearly half of which were Grapholitini, i.e., 46 species (45% of the total) (Table 2); and nearly 75% of all reared tortricid specimens were members of this tribe.

    A survey of fruit- and seed-feeding insects in Wanang, Papua New Guinea yielded results that deviate considerably from the pattern observed elsewhere. Of approximately 860 total specimens of Tortricidae representing about 44 morphospecies, nine species (20% of the total) and 398 specimens (46% of the total) were Grapholitini (Table 2). A partial explanation for the overall lower percentages is suggested by the following observations. During this survey seven species of Archipini (nearly as many species of Grapholitini) were recorded; Archipini are typically leafrollers, and five of the seven were represented by single individuals. The larvae of few, if any, of these species are obligate fruit-feeders, with most feeding opportunistically on fruit; e.g., from adjacent leaves. The survey also produced large numbers of Hilarographini (205 specimens, representing three species), a small tribe that is rarely encountered in surveys. Taken together, these two groups substantially diminish the overall percentages of Grapholitini species and specimens in the Papua New Guinea samples.

    Worldwide, Grapholitini include many important pests of fruit and seeds, including codling moth (Cydia pomonella (Linnaeus)), Oriental fruit moth (Grapholita molesta Busck), filbert worm (Cydia latiferreana (Walsingham)), macadamia nut-borer (Cryptophlebia ombrodelta (Lower)), false codling moth (Thaumatotibia leucotreta (Meyrick)), citrus fruit borer (Gymnandrosoma aurantianum Lima), and many others. Although most of these pests are limited to one or a few plant families, species in a few genera (e.g., Thaumatotibia and Cryptophlebia) are highly polyphagous. In the Kenya study (Brown et al. 2014), Thaumatotibia leucotreta was the most polyphagous species among all reared tortricids, recorded from 40 different host plants encompassing 12 different plant families. In contrast, most species of other Grapholitini genera (e.g., Cydia, Fulcrifera, Grapholita, Eucosmocydia, Camptrodoxa, and Thylacagastor) reared during that study were recorded from a single plant family, often a single genus or species, suggesting a high degree of host fidelity.
