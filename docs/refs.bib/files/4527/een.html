Are ants botanists? Ant associative learning of plant chemicals mediates foraging for carbohydrates

Abstract

1. Although associative learning is widespread across animals, its ecological importance is difficult to assess because learning is rarely studied in the field, where informative cues are juxtaposed against complex backgrounds of uninformative noise.

2. Ants rely heavily on chemical cues for foraging and engage in many ecologically important interactions with plants. Nevertheless, little is known about the role of associative learning of plant chemicals in ant foraging for carbohydrates.

3. In a field setting, the present study investigated whether the distantly related ant species Formica podzolica (Formicinae subfamily) and Tapinoma sessile (Dolichoderinae subfamily) exhibited associative learning of the chemical cues from two cooccurring plant species that are taxonomically and chemically distinct (Asteraceae: Helianthella quinquenervis and Apiaceae: Ligusticum porteri).

4. For two consecutive summers, ants were trained to forage from artificial sugarrich baits associated with the leaf chemicals from either H. quinquenervis or L. porteri for 24h, after which a twochoice test was deployed to assess whether ants would be more likely to select baits associated with the same (versus different) plant chemicals on which they had been trained.

5. The present study demonstrates associative learning of chemicals from both plant species, and these effects were consistent between ant species and years; training increased bait occupancy from 42% on the untrained scent to 66% on the trained scent. These results indicate that associative odourlearning may be widespread across ants and serve as an important mechanism mediating ant selection of resources.

Introduction

Associative learning, comprising the formation of predictive relationships between contingent stimuli in the environment, is widespread across animals and likely has farreaching ecological and evolutionary consequences (Giurfa, 2007; Ginsburg & Jablonka, 2010; MorandFerron, 2017). Associative learning can result in adaptive behavioural changes and increased foraging efficiency, such as when birds develop search images for cryptic moth prey (Tinbergen, 1960; Pietrewicz & Kamil, 1979), when bees form associations between floral colour and nectar rewards (von Frisch, 1914; Gumbert, 2000) and when parasitoid wasps learn to locate prey based on olfactory cues (Ishii & Shimada, 2010). In turn, associative learning is considered to result in the evolution of new traits (e.g. antipredator defences in prey) (Ginsburg & Jablonka, 2010) and to structure populations and communities of associated species (Ishii & Shimada, 2010). For example, search images in birds can result in frequencydependent foraging, with birds increasing their consumption of abundant prey but decreasing their consumption of rare prey, thus maintaining colour polymorphisms and species coexistence in cryptic moths (Bond & Kamil, 1998).

However, because most studies of associative learning are conducted in the laboratory, we often lack information about the ecological and evolutionary relevance of associative learning under field conditions (Pritchard et al., 2016; MorandFerron, 2017). Although laboratory tests of learning allow for greater experimental control, animals have access to different information and are under different selective pressures in the wild (Pritchard et al., 2016; Muth et al., 2018). As a result, estimates of learning performance in the laboratory may not translate to the field. Although recent studies provide fieldbased evidence for associative learning in both vertebrates (MorandFerron et al., 2015) and invertebrates (Huber & Knaden, 2018), additional field studies are needed to understand the extent to which associative learning influences species interactions in the wild.

Because ants rely heavily on chemical cues when foraging and engage in many ecologically important interactions with plants, associative learning of plant chemicals could mediate ant discovery and selection of plantassociated resources (Nelson et al., 2019). Ants have widespread effects in most terrestrial ecosystems, where they engage with plants by visiting flowers, dispersing and preying on seeds, cutting leaves, protecting plant and insectherbivore mutualists from their natural enemies, and serving as prey for carnivorous plants (Nelson et al., 2019). Such encounters with plants are likely the result of ants using both pheromonal (Morgan, 2009) and nonpheromonal (hereafter ambient) chemicals in the environment to navigate and locate resources (Knaden & Graham, 2016). Compared with other insects such as honeybees, for which associative odourlearning has been extensively studied (Giurfa & Sandoz, 2012), ants are known to have larger and more complex olfactory regions of the brain, highlighting the centrality of chemical detection to ant foraging (Gronenberg, 1996). All below and aboveground plant tissues are known to produce ambient chemicals, including both volatile and nonvolatile compounds on tissue surfaces, and ants could exhibit both innate (Edwards et al., 2006; Dttilo et al., 2009) and learned responses to these chemicals. Interestingly, because ants live in colonies, associative learning by individual ant foragers could be socially transmitted not only through the recruitment of additional foragers with pheromone trails, but also through mouthtomouth food exchange (trophallaxis) in which otherwise nave foragers learn to associate rewards with the chemical cues of the exchanged food (Provecho & Josens, 2009; Hayashi et al., 2017). Thus, although associative odourlearning in ants has received less attention compared with in other animals (Giurfa, 2007), it may commonly mediate ant interactions with plants.

Past studies provide evidence for visual and olfactory learning by ants (Roces, 1994; Harris et al., 2005; Dupuy et al., 2006; Narendra et al., 2007; Bos et al., 2010) but, to our knowledge, no studies have tested whether associative learning of natural blends of plant chemicals by ants mediates their associations with plants in the field. Associative learning has only been studied in a few ant species (Acromyrmex lundi, Camponotus spp. and Cataglyphis fortis) (Roces, 1994; Helmy & Jander, 2003; Huber & Knaden, 2018), thus making it unclear whether learning is widespread across ants. Most of these studies have been conducted in the laboratory, in which ants have been shown to form positive and negative associations with pure chemical compounds (Dupuy et al., 2006; Josens et al., 2009), artificial odours (Roces, 1990, 1994) and the cuticular hydrocarbon profiles of other ants (Bos et al., 2010). Such experiments provide opportunity for controlled tests of ant associative learning and its underlying mechanisms (e.g. individual versus social learning), although they lack ecological complexity and realism. Fieldbased studies have also shown that ants learn food odours (Helmy & Jander, 2003; Huber & Knaden, 2018) and use scents as landmarks for navigation (Wolf & Wehner, 2000; Steck et al., 2009; Buehlmann et al., 2015). However, most of these studies tested for associative learning using single chemical compounds, rather than natural blends of chemicals present in the environment (Steck et al., 2009; Buehlmann et al., 2015; Huber & Knaden, 2018). Although several studies indicate that ants may associatively learn natural blends of plant chemicals, evidence is scant and inconclusive. For example, one study provides preliminary evidence that ants associatively learn plant odours (Helmy & Jander, 2003), although foragers were assayed from only two replicate colonies, and training was performed on the colony surface where competing chemical stimuli were likely minimal. Similarly, although several fieldbased studies have shown that ants recruit faster and forage more efficiently on plantbased resources (seeds or leaves) that are more familiar, the mechanisms (e.g. ant odourlearning) driving these patterns were not assessed (Fowler, 1982; Johnson, 1991; Howard et al., 1996). Thus, additional studies are needed to determine whether associative learning of plant odours by ants mediates ecologically important interactions between ants and plants.

In the present study, we assessed whether ants associatively learn plant chemicals when foraging for sugarrich resources in the field. Accordingly, we tested whether ants exhibited associative learning when trained to forage for baits associated with leaf chemicals from two cooccurring taxonomically and chemically distinct plant species (Asteraceae: Helianthella quinquenervis and Apiaceae: Ligusticum porteri) that both naturally produce sugarrich rewards for ants (extrafloral nectar and honeydew excreted by aphids, respectively) (Inouye & Taylor, 1979; Mooney et al., 2016). By testing for associative learning of chemicals from both plant species by two ecologically and taxonomically distinct ant species (Dolichoderinae: Tapinoma sessile and Formicinae: Formica podzolica) across 2years, we provide evidence that associative odourlearning may be an important mechanism mediating ant foraging in the field.

Materials and methods

Study sites and natural history

The present study was conducted in July 2017 and 2018 near the Rocky Mountain Biological Laboratory (RMBL) in Gothic, CO (38.96N, 106.99W). In 2017 and 2018, we worked along seven and eight (respectively) transects in open, rocky meadows distributed within a 4km radius (15 total transects; coordinates provoided in the Supporting information, Table S1). In these sites Helianthella quinquenervis (Asteraceae; aspen sunflower) and Ligusticum porteri (Apiaceae; osha) are the two dominant herbaceous, comprising perennial plants that are both naturally associated with sugarrich rewards for ants (extrafloral nectar and honeydew excreted by aphids, respectively) (Inouye & Taylor, 1979; Mooney et al., 2016). Plants in the Apiaceae and Asteraceae family share many secondary compounds in common, including some aromatic essential oils and terpenoids in leaves and other plant tissues (Berenbaum, 2001). However, Apiaceae plants are known to have particularly distinctive odours and produce compounds such as furanocoumarins that are not present in the Asteraceae (Berenbaum, 2001). In the field, H. quinquenervis and L. porteri are distinguishable by scent (A. S. Nelson, personal observations).

The five taxonomic groups of ants that commonly occur in these sites are Camponotus spp., Formica podzolica, and the Formica rufa species group (subfamily Formicidae), Myrmica spp. (subfamily Myrmicinae) and Tapinoma sessile (subfamily Dolichoderinae). Ants in these three subfamilies have independently evolved to rely on sugarrich liquid rewards (e.g. extrafloral nectar and honeydew produced by hemipterans) as main components of their diets (Hlldobler & Wilson, 1990). Of the taxa in this community, F. podzolica and T. sessile are the most abundant (Mooney et al., 2016) and were most frequently observed in the present study. Formica podzolica and T. sessile have contrasting colony structures; F. podzolica forms large colonies (approximately 500040000 workers) with multiple queens that often occupy multiple nests (Deslippe & Savolainen, 1994; DeHeer & Herbers, 2004), whereas T. sessile forms small colonies (approximately 70 workers) with single queens that occupy single nests (Buczkowski, 2010). Although F. podzolica is a nonaggressive, competitively subordinate species (Savolainen, 1990; Mooney, 2006), T. sessile is more behaviourally and numerically dominant (A. S. Nelson & K. A. Mooney, unpublished data).

Experimental design

To test for ant associative learning of plant chemicals, we first trained ants to forage from artificial baits associated with leaf chemicals from either H. quinquenervis or L. porteri, and we then tested whether ants would subsequently be more likely to select resources scented with the same (versus different) plant chemicals in a twochoice test (experimental setup shown in Fig. 1). To train ants, we deployed 1014 artificial baits (hereafter training baits) along each of the 15 transects on one to three separate occasions (n = 200 total baits in 160 separate locations along the transects), with each bait separated by at least 10m to avoid repeatedly sampling ants from the same colony. To confirm that the 10m (minimum) bait spacing was sufficient to assay unique ant colonies, we measured ant foraging distances. In July 2018, baits (crumbs of pecan sandies of diameter 1mm; Kellogg Foods, Battle Creek, Michigan) were randomly distributed within a meadow actively foraged by both F. podzolica and T. sessile, the two most abundant ants in this study. When a bait was discovered (n = 17 and 10 total discoveries by F. podzolica and T. sessile, respectively), the foraging ant was then followed until it entered an active ant nest, and the linear distance from the bait location to the nest was recorded. On average, F. podzolica and T. sessile foragers travelled a meanSD of 1.31.1m and 0.90.3m from the nest, respectively. Accordingly, we assume that we assayed separate ant colonies at all locations along the transects. Ants were observed at 35 of the 70 total locations in 2017 and 71 of the 110 total locations in 2018. When we recorded ant species identities in the baits (performed in 2018 but not in 2017; described in more detail below), we observed two ant species at 19 of the locations and three ant species at 13 of the locations. Thus, we assume that we assayed a minimum of 151 unique ant colonies (although multiple species were likely also present at some transect locations in 2017).

To test for associative odourlearning, we used baits that were intended to imitate the composition of mutualistproduced resources associated with plants (i.e. extrafloral nectar or honeydew) and consisted of 10ml of a 20% solution by mass of forest honey (honey produced by bees that foraged on hemipteran honeydew; Langnese Forest Honey, Bargteheide, Germany) in 15ml plastic centrifuge tubes fitted with cotton wicks. We attached training baits to the top of 10cm sanded and lacquered bamboo sticks, which were used as artificial plant stems. We rubbed the sticks once with fresh leaves from either H. quinquenervis or L. porteri (alternating between species along the transect) when baits were first deployed. Leaves were locally collected from plants that visually appeared to be undamaged by herbivores. Because leaves were crushed in the process of rubbing them along bamboo sticks, they may have released additional chemical compounds compared with undamaged leaves. However, because plants often release additional chemical compounds in response to natural herbivore damage (Dicke & Baldwin, 2010), these chemical compounds would likely still be naturally available to be detected by ants. Moreover, we used bamboo sticks (that were presumably odourless) rather than natural plant stems so that other nonolfactory cues (e.g. textural or visual) from H. quinquenervis and L. porteri would not affect ant behaviour.

After training baits were deployed for 24h, we deployed a twochoice test within 50cm of each training bait to assess whether ants would be more likely to select baits associated with leaf chemicals from the same (versus different) plant species on which they had previously been trained (Fig. 1). The base and arms of the twochoice test consisted of bamboo sticks (length 10cm), with the arms positioned at a 90 angle. We placed one honey bait at the end of each arm of the twochoice test, randomly selecting one arm to rub with H. quinquenervis leaves and the other to rub with L. porteri leaves. The base of the twochoice test was unscented. We removed any vegetation that was touching the twochoice test so that ants could only access the baits by ascending the base and subsequently selecting one of the arms. Every 1020min for the subsequent 23h, we recorded whether ants were present (in both years) and their identities (in 2018 only), both in the baits and ascending the arms of the twochoice test. We considered baits to have been occupied by ants if ants were ever observed feeding in the baits. Although any ant could ascend the twochoice test (whether or not it had previously been trained), it is likely that ants in most locations had received prior training (with 94% of the training baits having been discovered by ants).

Statistical analysis

We used two separate models to test for associative learning (i) by two ant species (F. podzolica and T. sessile) that were most commonly observed in 2018 and (ii) by all ant taxa combined in both 2017 and 2018 (after excluding locations where ants were never observed feeding from any baits). For both tests, we used generalised linear mixed models (GLMM) with binomial distributions to assess whether bait selection depended on the main effects of bait scent and whether ants had been trained on the same (versus different) scent (hereafter sameodour training), as well as the bait scentsameodour training interaction. A significant positive main effect of sameodour training would demonstrate associative learning. A significant main effect of bait scent would indicate that there were different responses to the two plant species, regardless of experimental training. Finally, a significant bait scentsameodour training interaction would indicate that associative learning of one plant species was stronger than of the other. The model testing for learning in the two ant species in 2018 also included the main effect of ant species, as well as all two and threeway interactions, to test whether learning was ant speciesdependent. The model testing for learning in all ant taxa combined across both years included the main effect of year, as well as all two and threeway interactions, to test whether learning varied between years. Both models included site as a random effect. Because of low replication within transects (with only one or two baits being discovered by ants in some transects), it was not possible to include transect (n = 15) as a random effect (models were overfitted; not enough degrees of freedom). Moreover, the biotic and abiotic factors that might influence our results (including aspect, plant community composition and soil type) varied at a scale larger than the individual transect; some transects neighboured others and were essentially in the same biotic and abiotic setting. Thus, we grouped the transects into seven distinct sites, and we included site as a random effect in both models.

All analyses were conducted in r, version 3.3.2 (R Core Team, 2016). We constructed GLMMs using the glmer() function in the lme4 package (Bates et al., 2015). To test for the significance of effects, we conducted Wald chisquared tests with type III sums of squares using the Anova() function in the car package (Fox & Weisberg, 2010).

Results

In the model testing for learning by F. podzolica and T. sessile in 2018, there was a significant positive effect of sameodour training (GLMM: 2 = 5.30, P = 0.021), where ants selected a greater proportion of baits scented with the plant chemicals on which they had been trained; for both ant species combined, sameodour training increased bait occupancy from 45% to 66% (Fig. 2). The main effects of bait scent and ant species were not statistically significant (P>0.09 in both cases) and neither were any of the two or threeway interactions (P>0.60 in all cases). Accordingly, we found evidence for associative learning that did not detectably differ with ant species or plant scent.

In turn, we tested for interannual variation (2017 vs. 2018) in associative learning across all ant taxa in the community combined. There was a significant main effect of sameodour training (GLMM: 2 = 15.20, P<0.001), demonstrating associative learning across both years; sameodour training increased bait occupancy from 42% to 66% (Fig. 3). There was also a significant main effect of year (GLMM: 2 = 8.70, P = 0.003) and a marginally significant threeway bait scentsameodour trainingyear interaction (GLMM: 2 = 3.11, P = 0.078), with sameodour training increasing ant selection of H. quinquenervis more strongly in 2017 and L. porteri more strongly in 2018 (Fig. 3). None of the twoway interactions were statistically significant (P>0.25 in all cases) and neither was the main effect of bait scent (P>0.15).

Discussion

We have demonstrated that associative learning of natural blends of plant chemicals mediates the selection of carbohydrates by two ant species across 2years in the field. In 2018, two taxonomically and ecologically distinct ant species were both more likely to select artificial sugarrich rewards associated with the same (versus different) plant leaf chemicals on which they had previously been trained, and such effects did not detectably differ with ant or plant species; overall, sameodour training increased bait occupancy by these two ant species from 45% to 66%. Moreover, we found consistent evidence for associative learning with all ant taxa in the community combined across 2years (2017 and 2018); overall, sameodour training increased bait occupancy by the ant community from 42% to 66%. Thus, we provide evidence for associative learning of plant odours as a previously uninvestigated but likely widespread mechanism mediating ant associations with plants in a chemically complex field setting.

Despite F. podzolica and T. sessile being taxonomically and ecologically distinct, we found no evidence for differences in learning between these two species (2018 data only). These ants occupy separate subfamilies that independently evolved to rely on plantbased sugarrich rewards (Hlldobler & Wilson, 1990). Compared with F. podzolica, T. sessile ants have smaller brains (crosssectional widths of approximately 1 and 0.35mm, respectively) (Muscedere et al., 2014; Sulger et al., 2014) and live in smaller colonies (Deslippe & Savolainen, 1994; Buczkowski, 2010), although they also often recruit more workers to baits (A. S. Nelson & K. A. Mooney, unpublished data). Such differences in brain and colony size, as well as recruitment to resources, could have potentially altered the capacity for both individual and social learning in these ants. For example, ants from larger colonies are predicted to rely more on chemical communication (Beckers et al., 1989) and social learning could also thus be more important. However, to our knowledge, no studies have compared the neurophysiological bases of learning or the importance of social learning in these two species. Nonetheless, we found evidence for associative learning of natural blends of plant chemicals by both ant species (2018 only), as well as by all ant taxa present in the community combined (2017 and 2018). Thus, our results contribute to a growing number of studies of associative odourlearning in ants (Roces, 1990, 1994; Helmy & Jander, 2003; Dupuy et al., 2006; Bos et al., 2010; Huber & Knaden, 2018), which together show that associative learning of plant chemicals may be widespread across ant species.

There was a significant difference in learning across years (all ant species combined in 2017 and 2018) with respect to the two plant species, with associative learning being stronger for H. quinquenervis in 2017 but stronger for L. porteri in 2018. This bias in associative learning might have been a result of the ambient resource environment within which the experiment was embedded. Rewards associated with H. quinquenervis are more consistent throughout the season and across individual plants; all flowering H. quinquenervis produce extrafloral nectar in both June and July (Inouye & Taylor, 1979), whereas not all flowering L. porteri plants host aphids, and they do so in July only (A. S. Nelson, personal observations). Training from the ambient resource environment is possible because ant associative learning can be rapid (Huber & Knaden, 2018) and long term (e.g. up to 18weeks; Saverschek et al., 2010). Nevertheless, there were no obvious differences in the relative abundance of H. quinquenervis or L. porteriassociated resources between the 2years. Alternatively, the bias in associative learning might also have been a result of interannual changes in the nutritional demands of these ants. Ultimately, the difference in associative learning with respect to plant species is weak (e.g. too weak to be detected in the 2018 analysis) compared with the overall finding of strong associative learning for both plant species across both years.

Additional research is needed to determine whether and which plant traits mediate the strength of ant associative learning. For example, honeybees have been shown to be able to better discriminate between odours that are more dissimilar (Paldi et al., 2003) and it is possible that ants also exhibit stronger associative odourlearning of plants with more dissimilar blends of chemical compounds. In coevolved antmyrmecophytic plant associations, ant queens have been shown to use plant chemical compounds to distinguish between high and low quality host plants, both within and among species, as well as to select optimal hosts based on the amount of food rewards, nesting space, and previous damage from herbivores and pathogens (Torres & Sanchez, 2017; RazoBelman et al., 2018). However, it is unclear whether such responses to plant chemicals are innate or learned. In addition, the quality (i.e. composition of sugars and amino acids) and volume of sugar rewards can determine the strength of ant attraction (Blthgen et al., 2004; Blthgen & Fiedler, 2004) and could mediate the strength of associative learning, as has similarly been shown for honeybees (Wright et al., 2009). Thus, future studies should test for associative learning by ants of multiple plantassociated rewards (e.g. extrafloral nectar and honeydew) of different qualities. Because ants also use visual landmarks and tactile cues when navigating (Seidl & Wehner, 2006; Steck et al., 2011), future studies should test whether associative learning of multiple cues in combination mediates ant foraging for plantassociated resources (Fernandes et al., 2018).

In summary, we have shown that ants associatively learn chemicals from distinct plant species, and such learning may be widespread across ant taxa in the wild. In addition to associating with mutualist plants and insect herbivores, ants associate with plants in the contexts of floral visitation, seed dispersal and predation, leaf cutting, and plant predation of ants by carnivorous plants (Nelson et al., 2019). Thus, ant associative learning of plant chemicals may occur in many contexts (Roces, 1990; Roces, 1994) and mediate ant population and communitylevel effects on plants and the multitrophic communities in which they are embedded (Styrsky & Eubanks, 2007). As a result, ant associative learning likely has farreaching yet unrecognised effects on plant and insect evolutionary dynamics and community structure.
