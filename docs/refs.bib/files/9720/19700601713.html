    Technical assessment of Eucalypt pulps in the papermaking economy.

    Author(s) : Higgins, H. G.

    Journal article : APPITA, Melbourne 1970 Vol.23 No.6 pp.417-26 ref.31 refs.

    Abstract : The basic density (0.3-1.0g./c.c.) and extractive content of the wood are considered to be the main determinants of the pulping and paper-making properties of Australian-grown Eucalypts. Breaking length and burst factor decline steeply as wood density rises from 0.3 to 0.65 g./c.c., but decline much more gently at higher densities; the density of the paper made declines (and its bulk rises) over the wood-density range 0.3-0.65 g./c.c., but is then fairly constant. This behaviour can be correlated broadly with that predicted by a simple model involving the effect of cell-wall thickness on the number of fibres in a sheet of constant basis-weight, and on the extent of fibre collapse during the paper-making process. The economic potentialities and limitations of thick-walled fibres are considered on the basis of these and other criteria. The use of Eucalypts in Australia for the manufacture of packaging materials, fine papers and newsprint is briefly reviewed, and reference is made to possibilities for improving Eucalypt pulps by fibre blending and modified pulping procedures.KEYWORDS: Paper making, paper \ cardboard suitability \ species \ Pulp properties \ yields relation \ raw material \ wood anatomy \ chemistry \ Pulping, raw materials hardwoods broadleaved \ Pulping, raw materials suitability \ species
