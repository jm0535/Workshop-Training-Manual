ABSTRACT

Arguably the majority of species on Earth utilise tropical rainforest canopies, and much progress has been made in describing arboreal assemblages, especially for arthropods. The most commonly described patterns for tropical rainforest insect communities are host specificity, spatial specialisation (predominantly vertical stratification), and temporal changes in abundance (seasonality and circadian rhythms). Here I review the recurrent results with respect to each of these patterns and discuss the evolutionary selective forces that have generated them in an attempt to unite these patterns in a holistic evolutionary framework. I propose that species can be quantified along a generalistâ€“specialist scale not only with respect to host specificity, but also other spatial and temporal distribution patterns, where specialisation is a function of the extent of activity across space and time for particular species. When all of these distribution patterns are viewed through the paradigm of specialisation, hypotheses that have been proposed to explain the evolution of host specificity can also be applied to explain the generation and maintenance of other spatial and temporal distribution patterns. The main driver for most spatial and temporal distribution patterns is resource availability. Generally, the distribution of insects follows that of the resources they exploit, which are spatially stratified and vary temporally in availability. Physiological adaptations are primarily important for host specificity, where nutritional and chemical variation among host plants in particular, but also certain prey species and fungi, influence host range. Physiological tolerances of abiotic conditions are also important for explaining the spatial and temporal distributions of some insect species, especially in drier forest environments where desiccation is an everâ€present threat. However, it is likely that for most species in moist tropical rainforests, abiotic conditions are valuable indicators of resource availability, rather than physiologically limiting factors. Overall, each distribution pattern is influenced by the same evolutionary forces, but at differing intensities. Consequently, each pattern is linked and not mutually exclusive of the other distribution patterns. Most studies have examined each of these patterns in isolation. Future work should focus on examining the evolutionary drivers of these patterns in concert. Only then can the relative strength of resource availability and distribution, host defensive phenotypes, and biotic and abiotic interactions on insect distribution patterns be determined.

I. INTRODUCTION

Insect dominated plantâ€based food webs constitute an estimated 75% of global terrestrial biodiversity (Price, 2002; Mora et al., 2011). From an ecological and economical stand point, insects are the most important animals in terrestrial environments (Wilson, 1987). They include the most diverse and important pollinators (Bawa et al., 1985; Bawa, 1990; van Dulmen, 2001; Ollerton, Winfree & Tarrant, 2011), herbivores (Coley & Barone, 1996), and predators (Floren, Biun & Linsenmair, 2002) in the arboreal environment. Yet we have a poor understanding of how most arboreal insect species are distributed in time and space or the identity and extent of the causal factors responsible for those distribution patterns, especially in tropical rainforests where possibly 70â€“90% of species are unknown to science (Grove & Stork, 2000; May, 2000; Hamilton et al., 2010; Mora et al., 2011). Even for described species, we know very little regarding distribution patterns, habitat preferences, and the nature of their interâ€ and intraâ€specific interactions, or how any of these factors have influenced the evolution of biodiversity and foodâ€web dynamics, or generated current distribution patterns.

The spatial and temporal distribution of any species is the result of evolutionary tradeâ€offs of many selective forces (Wolda, 1988; Hawkins, Cornell & Hochberg, 1997; Denno et al., 2002). These include resource availability (Power, 1992; Price, 1992), plant biochemistry (Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Ehrlich & Murphy, 1988), biotic interactions such as competition (Denno, McClure & Ott, 1995; van Veen, Morris & Godfray, 2006), predation (Bernays & Graham, 1988; Ballabeni, Wlodarczyk & Rahier, 2001; Murphy, 2004), parasitism (Fox & Eisenbach, 1992), pathogens (Freed et al., 2005), and reproduction (Schal, 1982), and climatic or abiotic tolerances (Roubik, 1993; ThÃ©ry, 2001; van Klinken & Walter, 2001). The evolutionary outcomes of these tradeâ€offs are shown in three broad patterns that are a common focus of many studies (i) host specialisation, or the range of host species (i.e. plant, fungi, animal) particular consumer species utilise; (ii) spatial specialisation, or where in space, along both the vertical and horizontal planes, different species occur; and (iii) temporal specialisation, or when particular species are active. However, although similar selective forces are often proposed as causal factors generating these patterns, they are typically studied in isolation, and attempts to unite these patterns under a single theoretical framework are lacking.

In this review, I discuss each of the three distribution patterns and the selective pressures responsible for generating and maintaining them. I aim to show that each distribution pattern is not mutually exclusive and that similar selective forces are responsible for generating them and thus similar hypotheses can explain them. Specifically, I propose that insect species range from generalisation to specialisation with respect to each distribution pattern (host, spatial and temporal specialisation). Consequently, I argue that hypotheses put forward specifically to explain the evolution of host specialisation can be expanded to explain the generation and maintenance of all of the discussed distribution patterns. I advocate a holistic approach to examining arboreal insect communities with the understanding that each distribution pattern is part of a broad distribution pattern that is the evolutionary outcome of numerous tradeâ€offs between selective forces. I discuss the relative strength of selective factors and opposing hypotheses in the generation and maintenance of spatial and temporal distribution patterns.

II. HYPOTHESES FOR SPECIALISATION

Host specificity is a continuous measure that ranges from generalists that can utilise a large number of diverse host or prey species, through various degrees of specialisation on restrictive subsets of usually closely related species, to monophagous species that only feed on a single species (Jaenike, 1990; Barone, 1998; Lewinsohn, Novotny & Basset, 2005; Novotny & Basset, 2005). Similarly, other spatial and temporal distributions range from generalist (i.e. show no preference for particular localities in the environment or time periods) through to specialists (i.e. display a highly aggregated and predictable spatial and temporal distribution). While there has been much discussion and speculation as to the identity and importance of various selective factors to the generation of spatial and temporal patterns, only a small number of hypotheses are generally accepted or supported by empirical evidence. Here I focus on four prominent hypotheses to explain distribution patterns among rainforest insects (see Table 1 for a synopsis of evolutionary outcomes and testable predictions under each hypothesis). First I briefly outline each of these hypotheses before discussing them with respect to each distribution pattern. Throughout the review, there is a strong emphasis on the distribution of herbivores. This is because most arboreal insect species are herbivorous and herbivores have been the subject of most studies of canopy insects. However, the hypotheses discussed apply equally to species from other feeding guilds, with examples given where possible.

(1) Physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis

This hypothesis states that distribution patterns are the result of physiological tolerances among insects to their abiotic and biotic environment. When examined as a spectrum of specialisation, a generalist insect would be tolerant of a wide range of climatic conditions, such as temperature, humidity, and solar radiation, while a specialist would only be able to tolerate a small range of conditions, and would thus be restricted to areas and temporal periods when those conditions are met. It has been invoked to explain vertical stratification, edge effects, seasonality and diel activity among rainforest insect assemblages, where it has been suggested that these patterns are the result of adaptations to tolerable or preferable abiotic conditions (Didham et al., 1998a; Basset et al., 2003b; Didham & Springate, 2003).

Host specificity among herbivorous insects is often the result of adapting physiologically to the nutritional and defensive chemistry of host plant species. This hypothesis assumes that as insects become increasingly specialised to particular host plant species, they become less adapted to other potential hosts (Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Rhoades & Cates, 1976). Host plant use within herbivorous insect lineages is also predicted to be phylogenetically restrictive, such that the host plants of a particular herbivore consist predominantly of closely related species (Barone, 1998; LepÅ¡, Novotny & Basset, 2001; Novotny et al., 2002a,b, 2005; Kitching, Hurley & Thalib, 2003; Novotny & Basset, 2005; Ã˜degaard, Diserud & Ã˜stbye, 2005; Weiblen et al., 2006). Consequently, herbivore networks are expected to display a compartmentalised structure that reflects phylogenetic conservatism in host plant use within herbivore communities (Montoya, Pimm & SolÃ©, 2006). Equally, predatory insects may also display host specificity due to physiological adaptations to particular host species, or a group of closely related hosts (Dick, 2007; Paniagua, Medianero & Lewis, 2009). For example, some parasitoids adapt to the defensive chemicals sequestered in the tissues of their prey species (Gauld, Gaston & Janzen, 1992). The physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis is well supported by empirical evidence showing that insects are well adapted physiologically to the abiotic and biotic conditions in which they are found (Wolda, 1988; Jaenike, 1990; Basset et al., 2003b; Novotny & Basset, 2005).

(2) Enemyâ€free space hypothesis

This hypothesis states that the spatial and temporal distribution patterns of insects minimise mortality by predators, parasites or pathogens, or through intense intraâ€ or interspecific competition. Enemyâ€induced mortality in a particular time or place may be comparatively low because natural enemy abundances are lower, insects are better able to avoid detection by natural enemies (through crypsis or the use of refuges), or in the special case of hostâ€specific herbivores, because they can sequester secondary chemicals that act as deterrents to natural enemies. There is increasing support for this hypothesis among the literature to explain not only host specificity (Bernays & Graham, 1988), but also vertical stratification (Schulze, Linsenmair & Fiedler, 2001; Koike & Nagamitsu, 2003) and diel activity (Basset & Springate, 1992). While support for the importance of natural enemies for structuring the spatial and temporal distributions of insect assemblages is well founded, it is unlikely to operate in isolation. It is becoming apparent that the topâ€down influence of natural enemies often works in concert with the bottomâ€up influence of physiological tolerances and resource availability to explain current distribution patterns (Schultz, 1988; Denno et al., 2002; Strauss & Irwin, 2004).

(3) Neuralâ€constraints hypothesis

This hypothesis states that specialisation is the result of limitations in the ability of insects to process information about the identity or quality of potential resources, primarily for herbivores and their host plants (Bernays & Wcislo, 1994; Janz & Nylin, 1997; Bernays & Funk, 1999; Bernays, 2001; Janz, 2003; but see Wee & Singer, 2007). It assumes that specialisation results in faster decision times and fewer identification errors when choosing host plants (Bernays, Funk & Moran, 2000), which increases fitness by minimising timeâ€based mortality factors and avoiding inferior hosts. This is a relatively recent hypothesis but is gaining increased support among the literature. In particular, it has gained favour in some quarters because it can be used to explain host specificity among flowerâ€visiting insects, where the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis is unlikely to be supported due to a lack of herbivore defences within flowers compared to other plant parts (Wardhaugh et al., 2012; Wardhaugh, Stork & Edwards, 2013). This hypothesis may also be extended to include preyâ€finding by predators, in particular those that use herbivoreâ€induced synomones to locate plants under attack by herbivore prey (Dicke, 1999).

It should be noted though that the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis is a proximate explanation of host specificity (Singer, 2008), and the ability to locate and identify a particular host may have little bearing on the ability to consume that host or avoid associated natural enemies. Conversely, highâ€quality host species may be omitted from the host range of particular insect species because it is not recognised as an optimal food source. Phylogenetic conservatism is expected under this hypothesis because insects use chemical cues to discriminate between potential hosts and closely related host species are assumed to display similar volatile chemistry (Bernays, 2001). Thus, phylogenetic conservatism may be the result of phylogenetic constraints, whereby certain insect clades are only capable of recognising particular plant clades, preventing wider host utilisation. Unlike the other hypotheses, there is little scope for extending this hypothesis to explain spatial and temporal distribution patterns other than host specificity, although it may be important for explaining the vertical distribution patterns of some insect species (e.g. Berkov & Tavakilian, 1999).

(4) Resourceâ€availability hypothesis

This hypothesis states that insect distribution patterns reflect the availability of their desired resources. It does not however, predict that an insect's distribution will be directly proportional to resource availability, but rather insect abundance is expected to be proportionally higher on high resource concentrations, and proportionally lower on low resource concentrations than expected under a null model of strict proportionality. In this respect, the resourceâ€availability hypothesis displays close parallels with optimal foraging theory. The difference between the two is that optimal foraging theory is primarily concerned with the interaction between the availability and quality of different food types (Pyke, 1984), whereas the resourceâ€availability hypothesis as described here is largely restricted to the distribution of single food (resource) types, and is less concerned with variation in quality.

Resource availability is particularly important in explanations of insect seasonality (Didham & Springate, 2003) and vertical stratification (Grimbacher & Stork, 2007), but may also be important for the evolution of host specificity among some herbivorous insects (Moran et al., 1994; Kelly & Southwood, 1999), and potentially also those insects that prey on those herbivores (see Tylianakis, Tscharntke & Lewis, 2007). For instance, widespread or very abundant host plants typically support a richer herbivore fauna than rare host plant species (Futuyma & Wasserman, 1980), which is consistent with the prediction that insects congregate on abundant resource patches (i.e. common plant species) and poorly utilise rare or isolated resource patches (i.e. rare plant species). Like the previous hypotheses, the resourceâ€availability hypothesis predicts phylogenetic conservatism in host plant range among insect lineages. In this case, closely related host plant species can be viewed as closely related resources that can more easily be exploited than distantly related plant species. The resourceâ€availability hypothesis predicts that insect distributions will be optimised spatially and temporally, such that potential resources that do not coincide with peak availability will be underutilised.

III. HOST SPECIALISATION

Host specialisation by herbivorous insects has been attributed primarily to the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, with insects adapting to overcome the chemical defences of a particular host tree species, and subsequently losing the ability to overcome the defences of other potential host trees (Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Rhoades & Cates, 1976). This hypothesis has even been extended to explain the host specificity of parasitoids that attack herbivores that have sequestered the chemical defences of their host plants in their tissues (Gauld et al., 1992). Other factors such as enemyâ€free space (Bernays & Graham, 1988; Dyer, 1995), and local host plant abundance and plant community composition (resource availability) (Futuyma & Wasserman, 1980) may play an important role in forming and maintaining specialised relationships with particular host plant species. These hypotheses may also explain why many monophagous species do not utilise alternative hosts even though they are capable of feeding on them (Smiley, 1978; Futuyma, 1991; but see Wee & Singer, 2007).

All of the hypotheses predict phylogenetic conservatism in host plant utilisation, where coevolution has resulted in phylogenetic associations between host plant and herbivorous insect lineages. Examples of phylogenetic conservatism include such wellâ€known examples as fig wasps (Agaonidae) on fig trees (Wiebes, 1979) and Heliconius butterflies (Heliconiini: Nymphalidae) on Passiflora (Passifloraceae) vines (Smiley, 1978). Even for polyphagous herbivores, the identities of host tree species are not likely to be random plants from the local species pool (Becerra, 1997; but see Hulcr et al., 2007b), and a large proportion of insect species have been found to specialise on plants from within the same genus or family (Barone, 1998; LepÅ¡ et al., 2001; Novotny et al., 2002a,b,c, 2005; Kitching et al., 2003; Novotny & Basset, 2005; Ã˜degaard et al., 2005; Weiblen et al., 2006). Furthermore, taxonomically isolated tree species have been found to support fewer insect species or a greater proportion of specialists than tree species that coexist with other congeneric or confamilial species (Moran et al., 1994; Kelly & Southwood, 1999). For the physiologicalâ€efficiency and resourceâ€availability hypotheses, phylogenetic conservatism is the result of closely related trees being more similar in their nutritional quality and defensive characteristics than trees that are distantly related (Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Kennedy & Southwood, 1984; Futuyma, 1991). Similar chemical characteristics among closely related host species also constitute a central assumption of the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis, where host plants are usually identified via particular volatiles or surface chemicals (Bernays, 2001).

Host specificity is a relative measure that often varies at different spatial and temporal scales (Ã˜degaard, 2000a). The geographic range of a particular tree species rarely coincides with that of its insect fauna, and insects that feed exclusively on one tree species at one site, may feed on a completely different species at another (Fox & Morrow, 1981; Michaud, 1990; Thomas, 1990a; Novotny et al., 2005). This could be facilitated via any of the hypotheses discussed here, since none of them preclude local adaptations within species. However, host plant switching over evolutionary time should be expected to be more frequent or undemanding among ecological specialists [i.e. those that are monophagous due to factors other than physiological adaptations, such as local resource abundance or the search for enemyâ€free space (Smiley, 1978)] compared to obligate specialists under the influence of the physiological tolerance limits (Michaud, 1990; Futuyma, 1991; Radtkey & Singer, 1995). In this way, specialisation due to resource abundance or enemyâ€free space is likely more evolutionarily flexible than specialisation due to physiology.

There is growing support for the importance of the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis for explaining the evolution of host plant range. It is not enough that an insect species can thrive on a particular plant species, it also has to be able to locate that species among many other, often unsuitable, species. Therefore, rather than being restricted to particular plant species via speciesâ€specific defensive characteristics, some herbivores may be adapted to locating and identifying a particular subset of potential hosts (Bernays & Wcislo, 1994; Janz & Nylin, 1997; Bernays & Funk, 1999; Bernays et al., 2000; Bernays, 2001; Janz, 2003; but see Wee & Singer, 2007). A simple optimality model of host specificity assumes that the sensory systems [e.g. olfactory, visual, chemoreception (Prokopy & Owens, 1983; Visser, 1986; Dafni, Lehrer & Kevan, 1997; Chapman, 2003; Campbell & Borden, 2006; Powell, Tosh & Hardie, 2006)] insects use to select host plants are perfect, and that all host plant and feeding records are indicative of the natural environment. However, regional variation in host plant use (Fox & Morrow, 1981), as well as the omission of suitable host plants from an insect's diet (Stastny et al., 2006), or the inclusion of inferior plant species, suggests that insects can be confused during host selection (Fox & Lalonde, 1993; Janz & Nylin, 1997). Host selection mistakes have also been demonstrated among herbivorous species used as biocontrols (Marohasy, 1996, 1998). Indeed, host selection confusion has been proposed as a possible evolutionary mechanism for the inclusion of novel tree species in an insect's diet (Fox & Lalonde, 1993; Larsson & Ekbom, 1995), and is consistent with the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis for increasing host plant range.

It should be noted that there are also benefits to being a generalist, and the fact that most insect herbivores are not monophagous suggests that a more generalised host range is often favourable (Singer, 2008). In particular, a broader diet increases resource availability, although this paradoxically can be more difficult to locate or identify if neural constraints affect host plant selection. Since rainforest canopy herbivores typically feed on trees, an increase in diet breadth could effectively be an increase in habitat area. Species that exhibit aggregated distributions can be vulnerable to high mortality by natural enemies, so avoiding high concentrations of conspecifics by expanding the host range could increase survival (see Williams, Jones & Hartley, 2001). Generalists may display greater plasticity when selecting an optimal feeding site with regard to competition, predation, or unfavourable abiotic conditions, circumstances that are predicted under the enemyâ€free space and physiologicalâ€efficiency hypotheses, respectively. Furthermore, polyphagy will be favoured by migratory species, since different suites of host plants are likely to be encountered in different locations (Michaud, 1990). Dietâ€mixing is also predicted under the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis to reduce the build up of defensive chemicals and counteract nutritional deficiencies that may arise from singleâ€host diets (Held & Potter, 2004; Franzke et al., 2010; Karban et al., 2010). However, DNA barcoding has shown that some widespread, generalist species are actually complexes of multiple closely related hostâ€specific species (Hebert et al., 2004; Janzen et al., 2009, 2012; Valentini, Pompanon & Taberlet, 2009; but see Hulcr et al., 2007a). It therefore may be that generalists are rarer than is currently appreciated.

Host specialisation of herbivorous insects varies substantially depending on the resources being exploited (Novotny & Basset, 2005; Novotny et al., 2010). Some of this variation may be due to the differential herbivore defences employed by host plants in different plant parts (Gould, 1991; Merritt, 1996; van Dam et al., 1996; Hoy, Head & Hall, 1998; Veldman et al., 2007), and can thus be explained by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis. Under this hypothesis, host specificity should decline with decreasing levels of defence, which should in turn decline with the nutritional quality of the resources (Mattson, 1980), or their importance to the host tree's reproduction and growth. Evidence for such a pattern in tropical rainforests was provided by Novotny & Basset (2005) who reported that the percentage of species in different feeding guilds that are hostâ€family specific decreased in the order: granivores (99%) > leaf miners (96%) > fructivores (83%) > leaf chewers = sapâ€suckers (56%) > xylophages (24%) > root feeders (10%). Or, in terms of the resources that these insect guilds predominantly feed on: seeds > leaf mesophyll > fruit > leaf segments = sap > wood > roots. However, different taxonomic groups or feeding guilds that feed on the same resources (e.g. leaves) may exhibit markedly different levels of host specificity. For instance, caterpillars are usually quite specialised, while orthopterans are typically generalists (see Novotny et al., 2002b).

Apparent, longâ€lasting plant parts, such as wood, are characteristically protected via digestibilityâ€reducing compounds or extremely low nitrogen levels (Mattson, 1980; Neuvonen & Haukioja, 1984; Yamamura & Tsuji, 1995). Insects utilising lowâ€quality, apparent resources should be less specialised than insects feeding on highâ€quality, wellâ€defended resources because specialising on lowâ€quality food should not necessarily restrict an insect to one or a few related host trees. An insect capable of subsisting on a nutritionally poor diet can theoretically feed on any host species provided the material reaches some critical speciesâ€specific nutritional threshold (Mattson, 1980), and additional structural or chemical defences are not employed. Although host phylogeny may not be a strong promoter of specialisation for species that feed on lowâ€quality resources, specialisation may arise with respect to the physical state of the resource. For example, insects within the decomposer community (those that feed within rotting plants or animals) are usually generalists with respect to host species, but often specialise on particular stages of decomposition (de Carvalho & Linhares, 2001; Grove, 2001). Decomposer communities often follow a succession, with early successional species facilitating the arrival of later successional species. Specialisation among these species may be explained best by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, but is not precluded by any of the other hypotheses discussed here.

Insects that feed on wellâ€defended plant parts must overcome what are usually hostâ€specific chemical and structural defences (Iwasa et al., 1996; Grubb et al., 1998; Veldman et al., 2007), thereby reducing their ability to overcome the defences of other potential hosts (Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Rhoades & Cates, 1976). Variation in host specificity of insects utilising lowâ€ and highâ€quality resources was shown in a community of Lepidoptera, where monophagous and oligophagous species were restricted to feeding on flush foliage, which is often wellâ€defended chemically, while polyphagous species fed predominantly on mature foliage, which is better defended structurally (Cates, 1980). Unlike for lowâ€quality resources, specialisation among species that utilise highâ€quality, wellâ€defended resources is typically to host species, but is also best explained by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis.

Due to a lack of empirical data, there are several herbivorous guilds missing from the sequence compiled by Novotny & Basset (2005), in particular those insects associated with flowers. Some plantâ€“pollinator interactions are highly specialised, such as those between fig trees (Ficus) and what is usually a single species of wasp (Agaonidae) that is capable of carrying out successful pollination (Wiebes, 1979). However, fig trees are exceptional, and most rainforest trees are not limited to one or a small number of specialised pollinators (Bawa, 1990; Pellmyr, 1997). Although many trees employ generalist pollination systems that attract a broad range of local insects (Waser et al., 1996; Roubik, Sakai & Gattesco, 2003; Boulter et al., 2005), many of those insects may become specialised to the flowers of particular trees, despite the lack of reciprocal specialisation from the host plant (Ã˜degaard, 2000b; Wardhaugh et al., 2012, 2013). The host specificity of flower visitors, and the selective pressures that resulted in that level of specialisation, may vary depending on what resources insects are feeding on and the flowering strategies employed by particular plant species (Fenster et al., 2004). For example, specialisation among fig wasps may be best explained by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, whereas specialisation among insects that visit generalised flowers may be best explained by the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis (Wardhaugh et al., 2013)

IV. SPATIAL SPECIALISATION

The forest canopy is not a homogenous environment. Climatic conditions, such as light intensity, humidity, wind velocity, and temperature, vary from near ground level to the upper canopy and from the interior of tree crowns to the tips of the leaves, providing a series of often unique environments for plants and animals to adapt to or track (Nieder et al., 2000; Nieder, ProsperÃ­ & Michaloud, 2001; Szarzynski & Anhuf, 2001). The upper canopy for instance, experiences extreme temperature and humidity fluctuations, stronger winds, and heavier rainfall compared to the understorey. Different abiotic conditions within forest canopies subsequently affect the distribution of resources and the spatial composition and structure of the plant community, which has flowâ€on effects to insect consumers. Flowers, fruit, new leaves and areas of rapid growth such as vegetative and reproductive meristems are usually concentrated in the outer and upper canopy (Basset, Aberlenc & Delvare, 1992; Basset, 2001; Charles & Basset, 2005), whereas the interior mid canopy harbours the highest concentrations of shade leaves and vascular epiphytes (Nieder et al., 2000; Kelly et al., 2004), and the understorey is composed of saplings, detritus, and large amounts of living and dead wood. It is expected under the resourceâ€availability hypothesis that the distribution of insects would largely follow that of the resources they are exploiting, which are spatially separated (Schulze et al., 2001; Lewinsohn et al., 2005).

(1) Vertical stratification

Apart from examinations of host specificity, most other studies of the spatial distribution of arboreal insects have focused on vertical stratification (Basset et al., 2003b). Changes in insect community composition are often pronounced with height above ground, as are abiotic conditions, which are often concurrently measured and suggested as likely factors driving these patterns (Basset et al., 2003b). If true, vertical stratification could be explained largely by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis. However, I argue that stratified abiotic conditions are typically not the limiting factors restricting most insect species to particular vertical strata, but rather insects display vertical distribution patterns primarily as a function of the availability of resources (Basset, 2001; Roisin et al., 2006; Grimbacher & Stork, 2007; Paniagua et al., 2009), and can thus be explained by the resourceâ€availability hypothesis. But in some species, vertical distribution patterns may be the result of predator avoidance (enemyâ€free space).

Broad patterns in insect community composition are not consistent in different localities or host species, further complicating the search for general explanations for distribution patterns. Many studies have found that herbivore densities or species richness are higher in the canopy [Cameroon (Basset et al., 1992), Gabon (Basset et al., 2001), Panama (Basset, 2001; Charles & Basset, 2005)], whereas other researchers have found mixed results [subtropical and tropical Australia (Kitching et al., 1993; Grimbacher & Stork, 2007), Panama (Barone, 2000)]. These inconsistencies introduce uncertainty as to which hypotheses best explain vertical stratification patterns. Furthermore, the height of most rainforest canopies is not even and can vary considerably at a scale of just a few metres (Laidlaw et al., 2007). Therefore, in order to explain vertical distribution patterns adequately, studies need to be designed that specifically test the proposed hypotheses.

Apart from variation in abiotic tolerances, the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis may influence vertical stratification patterns in another way. Biotic and chemical conditions can vary within the same plant structures on the same plant species within different vertical strata. Leaves exposed to full sunlight typically have higher levels of nitrogen, carbon and secondary metabolites than shade leaves (Mole & Waterman, 1988; Nicholsâ€Orians, 1991; Brooks, Sprugel & Hinckley, 1996; Bond et al., 1999). However, other studies have reported mixed results (Le Corff & Marquis, 1999; Downum et al., 2001) and herbivory rates do not always correspond to leaf nutritional or defensive characteristics (Basset et al., 1992). Indeed, the chemical composition of leaves does not have a definitive effect on the distribution of many phytophagous insects, and other factors, such as structural defences, the presence, composition and abundance of endophytes, abiotic conditions and predator or competitor avoidance, may also be important.

Detailed studies on the vertical stratification of insects utilising resources other than leaves are uncommon. In New Zealand, Wardhaugh et al. (2006) showed that the density of the sessile barkâ€dwelling phloemâ€feeding scale insect Ultracoelostoma assimile (Margarodidae) was higher on branches in the canopy than low on the trunks of Nothofagus fusca (Fagaceae) trees. The authors suggest that vertical stratification in U. assimile is probably a function of bark thickness, which is vital to locating an appropriate settlement site. Thin bark does not provide enough crevices, which are preferentially colonised (McAllum, 1992), and if the bark is too thick, the insect's stylet cannot reach the phloem elements. Indeed, the lower trunks of smaller trees (10â€“30 cm diameter at breast height), which are approximately the same diameter as the heavily infested branches of large trees, also harboured high densities of U. assimile (Wardhaugh et al., 2006), indicating that vertical stratification was dependent upon resource accessibility/availability.

Several studies have examined the fauna inhabiting the suspended soils associated with epiphytes at various heights in the canopy (Nadkarni & Longino, 1990; Rodgers & Kitching, 1998; Winchester & Behanâ€Pelletier, 2003; Ellwood & Foster, 2004). For instance, the abundance of Thysanoptera, Formicidae, Nematoda, Collembola and Coleoptera in the suspended soils of an Ongokea gore tree in Gabon varied significantly with height (Winchester & Behanâ€Pelletier, 2003). Nadkarni & Longino (1990) found that the relative abundances of a number of insect taxa were similar in the canopy and on the ground, although densities were higher on the ground. This result is consistent with the resourceâ€availability hypothesis, which predicts that isolated resources (i.e. suspended soil) will not be as well utilised as large concentrations of similar resources (i.e. ground soil). However, abiotic and biotic conditions within suspended soils are likely to differ from those experienced on the ground, so physiological tolerances and predation may also be important.

The very low overlap in insect species composition found between the herbivorous faunas inhabiting mature canopy trees and conspecific saplings (Barrios, 2003) suggests that insects are not searching out a particular host species, but rather they are searching their preferred strata for appropriate host plants. This pattern is predicted under the resourceâ€availability hypothesis, where vertical strata that contain small quantities of resources are expected to be underutilised, while insects are expected to be congregated within vertical strata with abundant resources. However, seedlings may constitute a poorâ€quality resource for herbivores because they rarely flush with new leaves (Basset, 1999b, 2000). Therefore, avoiding seedlings in lower strata may be explained by the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, which predicts that insects will be concentrated on nutritionally superior hosts. Seedlings also lack a number of other resources that canopy trees provide, such as flowers, fruit, suspended dead wood and associated canopy flora such as epiphytes, and lianas. Clearly, most resources are not confined to certain strata, but most will be more predictable, relatively plentiful, safer to utilise, or of a higher nutritional quality in certain vertical strata than others. Selection will thus favour those individuals that restrict their searching to the discrete vertical layer of the forest where they will most likely locate the soughtâ€after resources, and not waste energy searching elsewhere (see Barrios, 2003). Such a pattern may also be consistent with the neuralâ€constraints hypothesis, if specialisation is the result of pressure to reduce searching times and energy wastage by restricting the search area to particular vertical strata.

The enemyâ€free space hypothesis has repeatedly been suggested as an explanation for vertical stratification of forest insects. There is some circumstantial evidence that pressure from aerial predators on certain insects, in particular large flying species like butterflies, may influence the structure of some canopy assemblages (Schulze et al., 2001; De Dijn, 2003; Koike & Nagamitsu, 2003). Conversely, in the rainforests of Africa, Panama, Papua New Guinea, and Borneo, small flying insects (that are potentially safer from hawking birds) were concentrated in the upper canopy (Sutton & Hudson, 1980; Sutton, Ash & Grundy, 1983). These explanations are largely speculative though, and delimiting the causal factors driving vertical stratification in these systems still requires vigorous hypothesis testing.

While I argue here that abiotic conditions are not as important as previously suggested with respect to vertical stratification, there are some instances where it has been convincingly demonstrated that the vertical stratification of insects is the result of speciesâ€specific physiological tolerance limits or preferences. For example, the flight activity of mosquitoes (Culicidae) in Columbia appears to be dependent on relative humidity (Bates, 1944). Large euglossine bees forage in the upper canopy to maintain a high body temperature, whereas smaller bees that are not as susceptible to heat loss are found in lower strata (Roubik, 1993). However, upper canopy tree species rely disproportionately on large bees for pollination (Bawa, 1990), while understorey species display more varied pollination syndromes (Bawa et al., 1985). So while large bees may be physiologically adapted to the upper canopy, this is also the stratum that contains the resources they utilise.

This example demonstrates the complexity of delimiting the ultimate factors that produced current distribution patterns when coevolution between the plants and insects is involved. Have the upper canopy trees evolved pollination syndromes that take advantage of the abundant large bees that are physiologically restricted to that stratum? Or did ancestral bees forage in the upper canopy because that is where their preferred floral resources were concentrated, with subsequent evolution leading to larger body size and physiological adaptations for that environment? Alternatively, another factor like predation may have been, or still may be, influential in producing vertical stratification in this system. Indeed, a higher proportion of upper canopy bee species display warning colouration to deter predators than species that forage in the lower canopy (Roubik, 1993).

The difficulty in proving that climatic variables caused vertical stratification is epitomised in a study by Dial et al. (2006) in Borneo. Among 14 vertically stratified arthropod taxonomic groups, 13 were positively related to leaf area (explained best by resource availability), 11 were positively related to climatic variables (explained by physiological tolerances or preferences), and 3 were positively related to height (possibly due to enemyâ€free space or even neural constraints). These examples demonstrate the need for manipulative experiments to test specific hypotheses, rather than sampling insects from different heights in a forest and correlating the distribution patterns with other abiotic or biotic variables.

Many insects take advantage of the abiotic conditions, in particular vertical strata, for resource or mateâ€locating purposes, even though these are not found in those strata. Many species use the canopy as a â€˜highwayâ€™ to move around the forest (Ã˜degaard, 2004), possibly because of more favourable abiotic conditions for flying in the canopy, such as higher visibility, or temperatures (Prokopy & Owens, 1983; ThÃ©ry, 2001). For small arthropods, the higher wind velocities in the upper canopy (Szarzynski & Anhuf, 2001) can aid in transport and locating potential mates or host plants via sex or aggregation pheromones and host plant volatiles (Schal, 1982; van Klinken & Walter, 2001). Fig wasps (Agaonidae) in Borneo use the higher wind velocity of the overstorey to detect the speciesâ€specific volatiles emitted by their respective host trees and to move quickly and efficiently over the forest (Compton et al., 2000). To locate the appropriate resources the wasps must utilise a vertical stratum that does not contain the desired resource, but does increase the chances of finding it. Therefore, this could be considered an example of vertical stratification in response to resource availability, since the wasps must use a particular stratum to find their preferred resources. However, use of the upper canopy to aid movement or resource location is not precluded by any of the focal hypotheses.

One of the few examples of vertical stratification that cannot easily be explained is the feeding pattern of Japanese beetles, Popillia japonica (Scarabaeidae) on linden trees (Tilia cordata). The beetles attack the uppermost foliage and defoliate the tree from the top down, but this pattern appears to be unrelated to any defensive or nutritional variable in association with the leaves, or any factor linked to beetle performance (Rowe & Potter, 1996). The authors suggest that the preference for attacking the upper part of host trees may be due to behavioural thermoregulation (i.e. tracking warmer temperatures in the upper crowns of trees), which is consistent with the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, or even host tree identification via its silhouette, which could possibly be due to neural constraints.

Vertical stratification has long thought to be a phenomenon that is synonymous with complex tropical rainforests (Terborgh, 1985), and studies carried out in temperate forests have often failed to replicate the findings of researchers in the tropics (Lowman, 1985; Winchester, 1997; Schowalter & Ganio, 1998; Le Corff & Marquis, 1999). Temperate forests appear to lack vertical gradients in microclimatic conditions within tree crowns, such as temperature and relative humidity, which are often pronounced in tropical rainforests (Basset et al., 2003b). This lack of variation means that temperate insects may not be able to track or adapt to the abiotic conditions within particular vertical strata. The absence of strong vertical gradients in microclimatic conditions would also influence the structure of the plant community (e.g. epiphytic growth) and subsequent resource availability. However, some temperate studies have found evidence of vertical stratification among their focal insect species, for instance, woodâ€boring cerambycid beetles in North American forests (Vance et al., 2003; Ulyshen & Hanula, 2007). In these examples, vertical stratification is likely due to resource availability, where species were more prevalent in the vertical strata that contained the appropriate larval resources; ground specialists develop in fallen logs whereas canopy species develop within twigs and cones in the canopy (Vance et al., 2003; Ulyshen & Hanula, 2007).

Data on the vertical stratification of insects in both tropical and temperate forests are relatively rare, and it is possible that the apparent differences in vertical patterns between these two systems are a sampling artefact. As species diversity increases, the differences in species composition in random samples will also increase. Since most tropical forests support a higher diversity of insects than temperate forests, samples collected in the canopy and close to the ground should perhaps be expected to contain a smaller proportion of shared species. If vertical stratification is largely restricted to tropical rainforests, this suggests that explanations related to the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis are particularly strong. But it should be reiterated that variation in abiotic conditions could also act as indicators of resource availability or enemyâ€free space rather than acting as physiologically limiting conditions.

Even within tropical rainforests, not all studies have demonstrated vertical stratification in their focal organisms (Intachat & Holloway, 2000), in particular adult Lepidoptera (Hill, Gillison & Jones, 1992; Schulze & Fiedler, 2003). Thus, strata specialisation may be more likely in some lineages than others, and is probably influenced substantially by the mobility and resource requirements of the insect taxa in question. High mobility, such as in many Lepidoptera, may facilitate the use of a greater proportion of the forest canopy, since little additional energy may be wasted searching a large vertical area than a small vertical area along the horizontal plane. Indeed, Thomas (1990b) found that monophagous Lepidoptera colonised isolated host plants as quickly as they colonised neighbouring host plants, which appears to contradict the resourceâ€availability hypothesis. However, â€˜isolationâ€™ is a relative term, and many strongâ€flying Lepidoptera may forage over a much greater area than most other insects.

Vertical stratification is the result of the interaction between resource availability, abiotic conditions, biotic influences and evolutionary history. Most strata specialists are probably restricted to particular strata simply because their food source is also restricted to or concentrated in the same strata, in support of the resourceâ€availability hypothesis. Other species may be restricted to particular strata due to intense competition or predation in other parts of the host tree, which is consistent with the enemyâ€free space hypothesis. In particular, the ant fauna of tropical rainforest canopies is highly structured, resulting in ant mosaics where dominant species are spatially partitioned due to competition for food sources (BlÃ¼thgen & Stork, 2007). The strength of the underlying forces responsible for the vertical stratification of insect communities has yet to be quantified, and it is possible that the influence of opposing abiotic and biotic interactions are forestâ€type, site or species specific.

(2) Horizontal distributions

Compared to vertical stratification, much less work has been carried out on the spatial distribution of arboreal insects utilising the same resources within the same vertical strata, either at the scale of individual trees or across a forest in general. Variation in the horizontal distributions of insect species or assemblages within individual trees is usually explained by factors in line with the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, such as variation in foliar chemistry due to spatial variation in sunlight and/or temperature (Maiorana, 1981; Mole, Ross & Waterman, 1988; Mole & Waterman, 1988; Moore, Myers & Eng, 1988; Nicholsâ€Orians, 1991; Bond et al., 1999). But results are variable, and there appears to be a lack of consistency across biomes in insect preferences for the sun or shade. While some have found higher densities of insects on the sunny side of trees (Moore et al., 1988; Basset, 1991c; Stork et al., 2001; Unsicker & Mody, 2005), others have found higher densities in the shade (Maiorana, 1981; Mole & Waterman, 1988; Mole et al., 1988; Nicholsâ€Orians, 1991). Not surprisingly, studies that report higher insect abundances on sun leaves are typically undertaken in temperate (Moore et al., 1988; Stork et al., 2001) or subtropical (Basset, 1991c) forests, while those that report higher abundances on shade leaves are usually situated in hotter tropical forests (Mole & Waterman, 1988; Mole et al., 1988; Nicholsâ€Orians, 1991). Spatial variation in abundance for many species therefore, could be related more to differences in abiotic conditions than differences in chemical or nutritive components of the foliage. Indeed, feeding trials showed that plants grown in full sunlight were preferred by generalist herbivores among several temperate plant species in North America, but shaded leaves suffered higher herbivore damage (Maiorana, 1981).

There is a lack of studies examining horizontal distribution patterns of canopy insects at larger spatial scales as well. While studies on spatial distribution patterns of insects in relation to forest edges are relatively common for groundâ€based species (Davies & Margules, 1998; Didham et al., 1998a,b; Estrada et al., 1998; Carvalho & Vasconcelos, 1999), studies examining edge sensitivity among canopy insects are rare, especially in tropical rainforest biomes (Malcolm, 1997; Foggo et al., 2001). Although empirical evidence is scarce, it is hypothesised that canopy species, such as lightâ€loving butterflies (Leidner, Haddad & Lovejoy, 2010), may be less sensitive to edge effects than groundâ€dwelling species due to the similar abiotic conditions of the upper canopy and forest edge (Foggo et al., 2001). But while many canopy insects may be preâ€adapted to tolerate the abiotic conditions of the forest edge, important species that they interact strongly with or rely upon may be highly sensitive to edge environments (Didham & Lawton, 1999; Laurance, 2000). For example, some plants such as lianas (Laurance et al., 2001) and early successional trees (Laurance et al., 2006), and important predators such as gapâ€favouring birds (Laurance, 2004), are more abundant at forest edges. It is likely therefore, that substantial changes in canopy insect community structure occur with proximity to the forest edge due to changes to higher order biotic interactions (Laurance, 2000; Foggo et al., 2001). Moreover, these changes are likely to be influenced markedly by local processes such as how and when the edge formed, edge shape, faunal and floral composition and their interactions, and the synergistic effects of continuing disturbance (Didham & Lawton, 1999; Laurance, 2000; Laurance et al., 2002, 2011). Therefore, resourceâ€availability and enemyâ€free space hypotheses may be more important, and the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis may be less important, for explaining changing distribution patterns of canopy insects with distance from the forest edge than for insects inhabiting the ground.

V. TEMPORAL CHANGES IN ARTHROPOD ACTIVITY

In addition to dividing resources in space, insects will often only be active at certain times of the day or year, introducing a temporal aspect to insect community dynamics. Activity and abundance patterns can occur over temporal periods as short as 1 day to the supraâ€annual periodicity seen in some arthropods (Didham & Springate, 2003). The most familiar example of the latter pattern is the 13 and 17 year cycles of periodical cicadas (Koenig & Liebhold, 2013), but the phenomenon has been little investigated among less conspicuous insects (but see Stork & Hammond, 2013), or those within highly diverse tropical rainforests where longâ€term seasonality studies are lacking (Didham & Springate, 2003; but see Wolda, 1989, 1992; Grimbacher & Stork, 2009).

(1) Seasonality

There is a lack of consistency in the use of the term â€˜seasonalityâ€™ among the entomological literature (Wolda, 1988). It is typically used to describe either the activity period of adults (Frith & Frith, 1985; Wolda, 1989, 1992; Grimbacher & Stork, 2009) or the feeding period of larvae (NiemelÃ¤ & Haukioja, 1982; Hunter, 1992; Forkner et al., 2008). However, there is general agreement that seasonality is a description of temporal synchronicity in life history (Wolda, 1988). Highly seasonal species display synchronised, predictable, populationâ€wide reproductive patterns, whereas aseasonal species display temporally overlapping reproduction. Consequently, only one or two lifeâ€history stages may be present in the population at any one time for highly seasonal species, whereas every lifeâ€history stage may be present and active for aseasonal species. Longâ€lived species introduce uncertainty to seasonality patterns, but temporal peaks may still occur in activity or dispersal throughout the lives of individual cohorts, especially if much of that time is spent in diapause or aestivation (Tauber & Tauber, 1976).

Insect seasonality is well known in temperate regions where food availability and tolerable climatic conditions are usually restricted to short, predictable windows (Feeny, 1970; NiemelÃ¤ & Haukioja, 1982; Wolda, 1988; Hunter, 1992; Forkner et al., 2008). Seasonality in these regions is therefore consistent with the physiologicalâ€efficiency and resourceâ€availability hypotheses. By contrast, seasonality is often disregarded in apparently aseasonal tropical forests (see Didham & Springate, 2003). But studies that have documented the population dynamics of insects within tropical rainforests have found that the abundances or activity periods of most species show strong seasonal changes (Wolda, 1992; Novotny & Basset, 1998; Wagner, 2001; but see Novotny et al., 2002c). Most tropical forests experience distinct seasons with respect to rainfall, usually referred to as the wet season (summer) and the dry season (winter), that many rainforest plants and their respective herbivorous insect faunas track (Tauber & Tauber, 1976; Denlinger, 1986). Many insect species are also highly sensitive to even small changes in photoperiod or temperature (Paarmann, 1974). Seasonal changes in abiotic conditions also influence plant phenology, and rainforest trees typically produce new leaves, flowers and fruit during relatively short periods of time, and continuous leaf production and/or reproduction is rare (Murali & Sukumar, 1993; van Schaik, Terborgh & Wright, 1993; Chapman et al., 1999; Forget, Kitajima & Foster, 1999; Poulin et al., 1999).

Discerning the causal factor(s) driving insect seasonality in tropical rainforests is difficult since insect abundance often correlates with abiotic factors such as temperature, solar irradiance and rainfall, as well as resourceâ€based factors such as host tree growth and reproduction (Frith & Frith, 1985; Intachat, Holloway & Staines, 2001; Didham & Springate, 2003; Boulter, Kitching & Howlett, 2006). Accordingly, insect abundance has been shown to increase in the summer wet season in Panama (Aide, 1993; Barone, 2000), Guyana (Basset, 2000), New Guinea (Novotny & Basset, 1998; Basset, 1999a), subtropical Australia (Basset, 1991b), Paluma in tropical Australia (Frith & Frith, 1985), and at the end of the dry season at Cape Tribulation in tropical Australia (Grimbacher & Stork, 2009). These peaks in insect abundance also coincide with peaks in the production of new leaves and/or flowers by many local tree species (Frith & Frith, 1985; Hopkins & Graham, 1989; Basset, 1991a,b; Bawa, Kang & Grayum, 2003; Boulter et al., 2006).

For many insect species in moist tropical forests, abiotic conditions, such as temperature, rainfall or photoperiodicity, probably do not physiologically limit the ability to grow or reproduce successfully, but rather act as reliable indicators of resource availability (Didham & Springate, 2003). As such, seasonality in tropical rainforests is probably a function of the periodic nature of resource availability (Basset, 1991a,b). Certainly, vertebrate seasonal migrations are invariably governed by changes in food availability (e.g. Kimura, Yumoto & Kikuzawa, 2001), and it should be expected that insects are also sensitive to such changes. Conversely, antagonistic and mutualistic insect species can influence the phenological patterns of host plants through temporal changes in the population dynamics of herbivores, seed predators and pollinators (Elzinga et al., 2007). For instance, dipterocarp tree species in southâ€east Asia temporally stagger their flowering, which maximises pollination efficiency by reducing competition and avoiding crossâ€species pollen transfer (Ashton, Givnish & Appanah, 1988). Hummingbirdâ€pollinated plants in Costa Rica have also been shown to display a temporally staggered flowering pattern (Stiles, 1978), as well as beetleâ€pollinated Annona spp. (Annonaceae) in Brazil (Gottsberger, 1989). However, these examples appear to be exceptional and little evidence has been found for competition or mutualisms among tree species for scarce pollinators that is strong enough to result in staggered flowering in other tropical (Boulter et al., 2006) or temperate (Kochmer & Handel, 1986) forests.

The availability of water is probably the most important limiting abiotic factor for species that are sensitive to climatic variability (Lieberman & Lieberman, 1984; Wolda, 1988; Frith & Frith, 1990; Kwok & Corlett, 2002; Didham & Springate, 2003), but there are exceptions (Wolda, 1989, 1992). As such, seasonality due to physiological tolerances is expected to be more pronounced in forests that experience more marked dry seasons (Wagner, 2001), and among invertebrate groups that are more susceptible to desiccation (Hurtado Guerrero et al., 2003; Palaciosâ€Vargas & CastaÃ±oâ€Meneses, 2003). Consequently, in tropical dry forests, where insect seasonality is increasingly under the influence of abiotic factors, plants often flush new leaves during the dry months when herbivore abundance is suppressed (Aide, 1992; Murali & Sukumar, 1993; Sloan, Zimmerman & Sabat, 2006; but see Lieberman & Lieberman, 1984; Wright & van Schaik, 1994). This phenomenon contradicts the resourceâ€availability hypothesis, which predicts that the peak abundance of newâ€leafâ€feeding herbivores should coincide with the peak availability of new leaves. In these environments, the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis provides a better explanation of insect seasonal patterns.

While strict seasonality may be a function of resource or climatic tracking, many species will opportunistically respond to large influxes of ephemeral resources by reproducing prolifically. For example, high supraâ€annual levels of flowering and flushing in southâ€east Asian dipterocarp forests are typically followed by large increases in the abundances of insects (Intachat et al., 2001; Itioka et al., 2003; Itioka & Yamauti, 2004). Such responses are expected under the resourceâ€availability hypothesis. However, the most wellâ€known supraâ€annual temporal patterns among insects, those of periodical cicadas, have been attributed primarily to predator satiation (Koenig & Liebhold, 2013), and can therefore be explained best by the enemyâ€free space hypothesis. However, cicada nymphs feed on xylem sap from tree roots, a resource that is extremely poor in nutrients (Mattson, 1980). Insects that feed on very poor diets often have prolonged juvenile stages, which is consistent with the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis. Indeed, other longâ€lived insects, such as woodâ€boring beetles, also subsist on poorâ€quality larval food sources, and their prolonged life histories may be explained best by physiological factors. But while poor nutrition may result in extended juvenile periods, the precise emergence every 13 or 17 years in periodical cicadas is clearly the result of selection favouring mass emergence, either to satiate predators (Koenig & Liebhold, 2013) or perhaps also to increase reproductive fitness (Karban, 1982).

(2) Diel activity

Most insects will only feed or be active at certain times of the day (Takeda & Skopik, 1997). There are a number of reasons for this, including minimising competitor interactions (Morris, Lewis & Godfray, 2004), predator avoidance (enemyâ€free space) (Basset & Springate, 1992), avoiding adverse environmental conditions (physiological efficiency) (Springate & Basset, 1996; Basset et al., 2001, 2003a) and maximising resource quality and/or quantity (resource availability) (Herrera, 1990; Armstrong, 1997; HernÃ¡ndezâ€Conrique et al., 2007). However, there is a general lack of consensus among studies as to the strength of selective forces driving diel activity patterns (Springate & Basset, 1996), and therefore, the identity of the appropriate hypothesis or hypotheses. In particularly, the role of predator avoidance remains controversial due to the contrasting results that have been found in different locations (Buckley, 1990; Basset & Springate, 1992; Novotny et al., 1999).

Other factors, such as tracking temporal changes in resource availability, could have a large influence on the diel activity of some tropical insects. For example, capture rates of pollenâ€feeding beetles within the canopies of Myristica insipida (Myristicaceae) trees in a tropical rainforest in Australia were positively related to the number of open flowers, which varied throughout the day (Armstrong, 1997). In Panama, two species of Dalechampia (Euphorbiaceae) flowered at different times of the day, which reduced crossâ€pollination from their shared pollinator, and was subsequently responsible for the resultant distribution patterns (Armbruster & Herzig, 1984). However, for many species circadian rhythms may simply be the result of evolutionary constraints (Kronfeldâ€Schor & Dayan, 2003; Boulter et al., 2005), such that diurnal species are unable to become nocturnal to avoid diurnal predators and competitors or utilise nocturnal resources and vice versa.

VI. COMBINATIONS OF DISTRIBUTION PATTERNS

The structure and distribution of insect assemblages in forest canopies are likely to display all of the patterns discussed. This is to be expected if each pattern can be viewed as a gradient from generalists to specialists and subsequently be explained by the same limited set of hypotheses. But do the discussed patterns correlate in predictable ways due to similar theoretical explanations?

(1) Host specialisation and seasonality

A positive relationship between host specificity and seasonality has been identified in some canopy insect assemblages, where monophagous herbivores tend to show strong seasonal changes in abundance and polyphagous species are more aseasonal (Novotny & Basset, 1998; Forkner et al., 2008) (Fig. 1). The degree of seasonality exhibited by an insect species is positively related to resource availability in time, which decreases with increasing host specificity. That is, highly seasonal insects tend to specialise on resources that are patchy in time, and patchiness in time increases with increasing host specificity. Indeed, the availability of ephemeral resources has been found to have a significant effect on the seasonal patterns of insect abundance (Basset, 1991a, 1997). The shorter the temporal window where the appropriate resources are available, the more seasonal the insect must be (Fig. 2A). This relationship can become so extreme that some monophagous insects have only a few days in which to oviposit on a suitable site for larval development (Hunter, 1992; Komatsu & Akimoto, 1995). Ovipositing too early or too late in such species can lead to complete reproductive failure (van Asch & Visser, 2007). Consequently, polyphagy tends to increase with the increasing unpredictability of host plant availability, whereas monophagy is more common on predictable host plants or plant parts (Cates, 1981; Forkner et al., 2008).

Polyphagous species need only to search, locate and move between trees in the local area that happen to bear the required resources to feed and/or reproduce (Novotny & Basset, 1998). Since resources are plentiful in time for a polyphagous insect, seasonality should be low or nonâ€existent (Fig. 2C). Along the continuum from specialisation to extreme generalisation, new leaves, and also flowers, fruit, and suspended dead wood, shift from being patchy to abundant in time in most tropical rainforests (Table 2). Oligophagous species, which feed on several host species, are expected to show an intermediate level of seasonality (Fig. 2B), since the temporal window where appropriate resources are available should be greater than for monophagous species, but may not be continuous as is the case for many polyphagous species (Fig. 2C).

At the local forest scale, few resources are likely to be patchy in time (Frith & Frith, 1985; Boulter et al., 2006), and only a few isolated examples of temporally patchy resources exist at larger spatial scales in moist tropical rainforests (Table 2). For example, the massâ€flowering events in dipterocarp forests in Southâ€east Asia: flowers, fruit and seeds within these forests are rare most of the time, and then become extremely abundant during the intermittent periods when most of the trees in the local area reproduce simultaneously (Ashton et al., 1988; Momose et al., 1998; Brearley et al., 2007). In these forests, marked seasonality in polyphagous insects could be more prevalent than in other complex tropical rainforests, since the resources available to polyphagous species display a similar patchiness in time as those available to monophagous species (see Ashton et al., 1988; Toy, Marshall & Pong, 1992; Momose et al., 1998). Most other tropical rainforests exhibit temporal peaks in leaf and flower production, but these tend to be rather shallow peaks in abundance (Frith & Frith, 1985; Boulter et al., 2006), and availability probably does not become so low during the troughs that polyphagous species will also be highly seasonal. Conversely, insects specialised to feeding on resources that are abundant in both time and space within host tree species and at a local forest scale, such as mature leaves, wood and roots, should also show less seasonality, since they are always available.

(2) Host specificity and spatial specialisation

As far as I know, no one has reported empirical evidence for a broad correlation between host specificity of herbivores and spatial specialisation, such as that shown for host specificity and seasonality. However, links have been recorded between the host specificity of the parasitoids of galling insects and vertical stratification in Panama, where canopy specialists were more host specific than species found near the ground (Paniagua et al., 2009), although it remains unknown why this is the case. Host specialisation can be viewed as an extreme form of resource specialisation. Since vertical stratification in insect assemblages is often a product of the vertical stratification of available resources, host specialisation may also correlate with degree of vertical specialisation in some other cases as well. For example, pollination systems in tropical forests can be vertically stratified (Bawa et al., 1985; Bawa, 1990), as are some pollinators (Roubik, 1993). It is thus possible for hostâ€specific flower visitors to be more stratified than generalists who visit flowers from a variety of tree species exhibiting different pollination syndromes.

It may also be possible for host specificity to vary with proximity to the rainforest edge if, for example, host specificity varied between insect communities associated with pioneer (edge) and persistent (interior) tree species. For instance, Basset (1996) found that pioneer tree species in Papua New Guinea supported a higher percentage of specialist species among the herbivore fauna (41.8%) than persistent tree species (33.54%). However, largeâ€scale patterns in host specificity with respect to distance from the forest edge will depend on a number of factors, including changes in the composition of tree species with distance from the edge, as well as how the edge formed (natural or human disturbance) and how long ago. Hypothetical patterns, if they exist, await future research.

(3) Temporal and spatial specialisation

Abiotic conditions are continually changing through time, so a pattern like vertical stratification, which is often related to the climatic conditions in different vertical strata either directly or via resource distribution, can also change through time. An extreme example of seasonal vertical migration in tropical rainforests occurs in the seasonally inundated forests of the Amazon basin (Adis, Lubin & Montgomery, 1984). In response to the flooding of the forest floor, groundâ€strata arthropods, such as many spiders and beetles, move into the trees to avoid drowning (Marques et al., 2006). In forests that do not experience seasonal flooding, temporal changes in vertical stratification are undoubtedly less extreme, but may still be widespread. Berkov & Tavakilian (1999) found that xylophagous beetles (Palame spp., Cerambycidae) utilising Lecythidaceae trees in French Guiana reproduced exclusively in the canopy in the wet season, with reproduction at ground level restricted to the dry season. This pattern could be due to seasonal changes in enemy abundance (harmful fungi are plentiful at ground level in the wet season), or abiotic conditions (the ground is too wet in the wet season and the canopy is too dry in the dry season). In the dipterocarp forests of Sarawak, the abundance of Blattaria, Heteroptera and Coleoptera shifted to higher vertical strata in response to the increased floral or associated resources that are produced during generalâ€flowering episodes (Itioka et al., 2003). Thus, the insects in this example exhibit temporally dependent vertical stratification based upon the availability of appropriate resources. A similar explanation was given by Enders (1974) who found that the vertical stratification of two species of spiders (Argiope aurantia and A. trifasciata) in North Carolina was dependent on body size, with large mature individuals occurring higher than smaller immature individuals. The author proposed that this was due to differences in the size of potential prey in different vertical strata, where small prey items, and small spiders, were more plentiful among the herb layer than in the trees. Thus, there is a change in the vertical strata occupied by spiders over time as a function of growth and prey choice. Stork et al. (2001) showed that some beetle species occupying the canopies of oak trees in Britain changed their preferred side of the tree throughout the year. In one species, Adalia decempunctata (Coccinellidae), different colour morphs exhibited different spatial preferences. Different microsites will also be favoured by different lifeâ€history stages (larvae and adult distributions and food sources often differ), or during different parts of the adult life cycle (refuges used for diapause or aestivation may not be used as feeding or breeding sites).

Many species of insects will occupy different vertical strata at different times of the day. Some Diptera (Culucidae, Cecidomyiidae, Sciaridae, Chironomidae, and Ceratopogonidae) will swarm in the canopy at dawn and dusk, despite the fact that most of these species are associated with predominantly groundâ€based resources (Bates, 1944; Stork, 1991). This behavioural pattern may be associated with optimal climate tracking, where preferred abiotic conditions shift from the canopy to the ground and back to the canopy again during the day (physiological efficiency), or enemy avoidance (Stork, 1991; Didham, 1997). Basset et al. (2001, 2003a) showed that the species turnover between nocturnal and diurnal insects was greater in the canopy than in the understorey in Gabon. This pattern is consistent with the physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis, which predicts that temporal periodicity will be more pronounced in sites like the upper canopy, which experiences greater fluctuations in abiotic conditions compared to the understorey. Conversely, some species display daily rhythms in stratification due to vertically separated feeding sites and refuges (temporal changes in resource use) (Schal & Bell, 1986).

VII. EVOLUTIONARY FORCES DRIVING DISTRIBUTION PATTERNS

The previous sections have demonstrated that different distribution patterns are influenced in most cases by similar selective forces. Resource availability in particular is a major selective force influencing each distribution pattern (including host specialisation where host availability is synonymous with resource availability). Ultimately, insects require food and hence must spend at least some time with their food sources, and since those food sources also often double as substrates or habitats, it should be expected that the distribution of insects mirrors the distribution of the resources that they utilise. Thus, spatial and temporal aggregation patterns typically reflect the spatial and temporal distributions of resources. Resource availability defines the fundamental niche of insect species and as such can be considered the main driver of arboreal insect distribution patterns. Further biotic and abiotic factors act to refine the distribution of arboreal insects.

Of the important biotic interactions, coevolution between plants and herbivorous insects and the effect of natural enemies feature prominently in observed ecological patterns. There are many examples of plant antiâ€herbivore defences and counterâ€defences among host plants and herbivorous insects (e.g. see Ehrlich & Raven, 1964; Jaenike, 1990; van Dam et al., 1995; Coley & Barone, 1996; Hoy et al., 1998; Gatehouse, 2002; Agrawal, Lau & HambÃ¤ck, 2006; NÃºÃ±ezâ€FarfÃ¡n, Fornoni & Valverde, 2007; Roslin & Salminen, 2008; Futuyma & Agrawal, 2009; Ballhorn, Kautz & Lieberei, 2010). Moreover, since antiâ€herbivore defences are not evenly distributed within or between host plants (van Dam, Verpoorte & van der Meijden, 1994; Hoy et al., 1998), many studies have recorded distribution patterns among herbivorous insects that reflect variation in plant susceptibility to attack (e.g. Feeny, 1970; Haukioja, 1980; Forkner, Marquis & Lill, 2004). By contrast, other studies have identified distribution patterns among herbivorous insects that do not necessarily match the distribution patterns of host plant defences (see Smiley, 1978; Cates, 1980; Coley, 1983; Le Corff & Marquis, 1999; Peeters, 2002; Viswanathan, McNickle & Thaler, 2008). This lack of consistency suggests that interactions with host plants are more important to the generation of distribution patterns among some species or assemblages than others. A similar conclusion can be drawn for the effect of natural enemies on insect distribution patterns, where there is inconsistency in the role of predators in modifying herbivore distribution patterns (e.g. Buckley, 1990; Basset & Springate, 1992; Freeland & Boulton, 1992; Gauld et al., 1992; Power, 1992; Hawkins et al., 1997; Novotny et al., 1999; Ode, 2006).

Abiotic conditions can have a strong effect on temporal dynamics and some spatial distribution patterns in tropical rainforests because microclimatic conditions can vary considerably between day and night, different seasons, and between the ground and upper canopy (Wolda, 1988; Aide, 1992; Szarzynski & Anhuf, 2001; Basset et al., 2003b; Dial et al., 2006). Frequent correlations with biotic factors, such as resource availability, introduces doubt about the direct influence of abiotic factors on insect distribution patterns (Didham & Springate, 2003). Consequently, abiotic factors may be much less important than previously assumed as selective forces influencing arboreal insect distributions in the canopies of moist tropical rainforests (see Wolda, 1989). These factors are likely to become increasingly important however in systems that experience greater climatic seasonality, especially with respect to rainfall (Aide, 1992; Wagner, 2001; Hurtado Guerrero et al., 2003).

Clearly, every species of tree in tropical rainforests (or any other forest type) will not harbour the same number of insect species or individuals (e.g. Novotny et al., 2002a). The structure, architecture, growth rate, successional status, growth pattern, size, abundance, phylogeny, distribution, phenology, chemical composition, and the diversity, abundance and predictability of resources on different host species directly and indirectly effects the size and composition of the respective insect faunas (Coley, 1983; Lawton, 1983; Basset, 1996; Strauss & Irwin, 2004; Ricklefs, 2008). Coupled with the fact that many of these factors change through time, adding a temporal component to population dynamics, it is apparent that the arboreal insect fauna within tropical rainforests is an everâ€changing, dynamic mosaic of interacting species that are continually searching for and utilising the myriad resources available to them. Over evolutionary time, insects have adapted to the temporal and spatial heterogeneity in the availability of their required resources in order to maximise the chances of successfully locating an appropriate site for feeding and reproducing.

Determining the relative strength of host plant variables, competition, and natural enemies on herbivore population dynamics is not straightforward, with both plants and insects displaying several tradeâ€offs and coâ€correlated variables that introduce uncertainty (see Herms & Mattson, 1992; Eichhorn et al., 2007). Future studies are needed to determine the strength of each selective force in natural communities through experimental manipulations, as well as both longâ€term and largeâ€scale sampling programs. Indeed, it has been shown that to survey rainforest insect communities adequately requires longâ€term sampling from multiple vertical strata at multiple sites using multiple sampling methods (Adis et al., 1984; Kitching, Li & Stork, 2001; Stork & Grimbacher, 2006; Basset et al., 2012). Only then will it be possible to confidently quantify the forces and mechanisms structuring the distribution and structure of rainforest insect communities, enabling accurate predictions of spatial and temporal distribution patterns.

VIII. CAVEATS AND LIMITATIONS

While compiling this review, several limitations among the published literature have become evident. First is the decline during the last 10â€“15 years of empirical studies on the distributions of canopy Insects. I speculate that this trend is largely due to the short duration (usually 3 years) of most research grants, and PhD candidatures. Smallâ€scale, shortâ€term studies have provided enough data to discern patterns and speculate about the factors that may be responsible, but there is little more that can be learned from small â€˜patternâ€™ projects. The research preferences of entomologists are also a factor. So little is known about tropical rainforest canopy insects that it is often more appealing to move on to the next biggest gap in our knowledge than it is to satisfactorily address previous research questions. Indeed, while reâ€sampling to confirm previously identified patterns may be understandably rare, unfortunately so are studies that seek to remove the speculation surrounding explanations for insect diversity patterns. I suggest that what are needed are more largeâ€scale, longâ€term projects where researchers and students can tackle both exciting new research areas and thoroughly investigate hypotheses to explain previously noted distribution patterns that may have lost some of their â€˜wowâ€™ factor. Shortâ€term projects can also contribute if they are carried out in series or if several occur simultaneously, effectively piecing together a largeâ€scale, longâ€term project out of many smaller projects.

The last caveat that should be mentioned is the issue of phylogenetic constraints. Distribution patterns of some species may reflect tolerances or adaptations that are prevalent at taxonomic levels far above species. For example, flightless arthropods may be expected to be rarer in the upper canopy than on the ground, while small species with permeable exoskeletons may also be rare in areas or temporal periods that are particularly dry, due to their vulnerability to water loss. Other examples include nocturnal species being unable to adapt to diurnal conditions and vice versa, and species being restricted to certain groups of plants because of the difficulty in adapting to recognise (neural constraints) or detoxify (physiological efficiency) chemicals within other potential host plant lineages. It is unreasonable to expect any species to be able to adapt to exploit any potential resource. Ultimately, the evolutionary history of any species will always have a bearing on the current spatial and temporal distributions of extant species, and should be taken into account when testing hypotheses to explain distribution patterns.

IX. CONCLUSIONS

(1) Spatial and temporal distribution patterns among rainforest insects can be placed along a continuum from extreme generalisation [i.e. wide host/prey range (plant, fungi or animal species), wide spatial distribution, and no temporal peaks in abundance] to extreme specialisation (i.e. host/prey specific, spatially restricted, and highly seasonal or with strong circadian rhythms).

(2) Similar selective forces and hypotheses have been proposed to explain each distribution pattern. I argue that the same small set of hypotheses can be used to explain each pattern, and the challenge now is to test these hypotheses.

(3) Resource availability is the most important factor driving spatial and temporal distribution patterns among arboreal insect assemblages. Vertical stratification, horizontal distribution and some seasonal and diel activity patterns largely reflect where and when the appropriate resources are available. For some species, host specificity is also influenced by resource availability.

(4) The physiologicalâ€efficiency hypothesis is particularly important for explaining host plant/prey range, but physiological tolerances of abiotic conditions may be important factors influencing vertical stratification and temporal periodicity, especially in drier forests that experience greater variability in abiotic conditions in time and space.

(5) The role of natural enemies in generating insect distribution patterns is particularly speculative. It may be that the search for enemyâ€free space acts in concert with resource availability and physiological adaptations further to restrict spatial and temporal distribution patterns, rather than define the fundamental niche.

(6) Many previous studies have reported spatial and temporal distribution patterns and speculated as to which selective forces may have generated them. It is recommended that future studies test specific hypotheses to explain insect distribution patterns, especially if multiple patterns can be studied simultaneously.
