    Abstract

    1. Red wood ants are among the most numerous generalist predators and strongly affect the composition of arthropod communities in forest ecosystems. However, their trophic position remains poorly understood. Stable isotope analysis was applied to study the trophic position of Formica aquilonia and reveal seasonal changes in its trophic links with both myrmecophilous aphids and other invertebrates in a mixed forest of western Siberia.

    2. The Œ¥15N values of F. aquilonia exceeded those of herbivores and aphids by approximately 3.5‚Ä∞. Despite obligate trophobiotic relationships with aphids, F. aquilonia occupied the trophic position of first‚Äêorder predator. The higher content of 13C in the worker ants, compared with members of grazing food chains, was explained by their consumption of 13C‚Äêenriched aphid honeydew.

    3. Myrmecophilous tree‚Äêdwelling aphids were enriched in 13C and 15N relative to grass‚Äêinhabiting species, and the honeydew of tree‚Äêdwelling aphids had higher Œ¥13C values than those of the honeydew of grass‚Äêinhabiting aphids.

    4. The decrease in Œ¥13C values of the worker ants from spring and summer to autumn apparently reflected the transition from the collection of tree sap and feeding on the aphid honeydew from trees with high 13C content in the spring and early summer to a more diverse liquid diet in late summer, which included 13C‚Äêdepleted honeydew of aphids from herbs.

    5. The prevalence of the 15N‚Äêdepleted aphid honeydew in the ants' diet in the second half of the summer is discussed as one possible explanation for the seasonal decline in Œ¥15N values of the worker ants.

    Introduction

    Red wood ants (Formica rufa group) are among the most numerous generalist predators, strongly affecting the composition of arthropod communities in temperate forest ecosystems (Cherix & Bourne, 1980; Lenoir et‚Äâal., 2003; Reznikova & Dorosheva, 2004; Punttila et‚Äâal., 2004; Neuvonen et‚Äâal., 2012). These species are also herbivores, consuming honeydew of trophobiont insects (primarily aphids), plant sap, nectar and plant seeds (Horstmann, 1974; Skinner, 1980; Fowler & Macgarvin, 1985; Rosengren & Sundstr√∂m, 1991; Domisch et‚Äâal., 2009). Therefore, the ecological impact of red wood ants extends over several trophic levels and affects both grazing and detrital food chains (Mahdi & Whittaker, 1993; Laakso & Set√§l√§, 2000; Frouz et‚Äâal., 2008; Wardle et‚Äâal., 2011). Notably, despite many investigations of the diet of red wood ants, their trophic position within food webs remains unclear, possibly because of significant temporal dynamics and variability in their local food preferences (Rosengren & Sundstr√∂m, 1991; Domisch et‚Äâal., 2009).

    Application of stable isotope analysis (SIA) can help to determine the trophic position because this method can obtain time‚Äêintegrated information on animal nutrition (Gannes et‚Äâal., 1997; Post, 2002; Tiunov, 2007). The isotopic composition of carbon (13C/12C ratio, usually expressed as Œ¥13C) can be used to infer the Œ¥13C value of the primary sources of food because of the low trophic fractionation (ŒîŒ¥‚Äâ=‚ÄâŒ¥consumer‚Äâ‚àí‚ÄâŒ¥food) of 13C, which is estimated at approximately 0.5‚Äì1‚Ä∞ (DeNiro & Epstein, 1978; Post, 2002; McCutchan et‚Äâal., 2003). Œ¥13C has been used in many ecological studies, in particular to discriminate between diets of different species based on plants with different photosynthetic pathways (C3 vs C4; Gratton & Forbes, 2006). By contrast, the isotopic composition of nitrogen (15N/14N ratio, expressed as Œ¥15N) can be used to infer the trophic level of the consumer because of the pronounced trophic fractionation of 15N, which is estimated at approximately 2‚Äì4‚Ä∞ with each trophic level (Minagawa & Wada, 1984; McCutchan et‚Äâal., 2003; Vanderklift & Ponsard, 2003).

    Stable isotope analysis has been applied to several ant ecological studies in European and Mediterranean communities, but these studies do not include red wood ants (Ottonetti et‚Äâal., 2008; O'Grady et‚Äâal., 2010; Platner et‚Äâal., 2012). Data on the carbon isotopic composition of plants, arthropods and ants were used to reveal the contribution of different food sources derived from tree canopies and the herbaceous layer to the diets of ant species in Mediterranean communities (Ottonetti et‚Äâal., 2008; Platner et‚Äâal., 2012). The data on nitrogen isotopic composition show that most ant species in different ecosystems are omnivores and occupy an intermediate position between the specialised herbivorous and predatory groups of ant species (Davidson et‚Äâal., 2003; Bl√ºthgen et‚Äâal., 2003; Platner et‚Äâal., 2012; Pfeiffer et‚Äâal., 2014). In central Europe, the Œ¥15N values of Formica species (combined data from eight species) place them into an intermediate trophic position between predominantly ‚Äòtrophobiotic‚Äô, i.e. herbivorous (e.g. Camponotus), and predatory (e.g. Myrmica) genera (Fiedler et‚Äâal., 2007).

    The diet of red wood ants is complex, which leads to the hypothesis that species in this group also occupy an intermediate trophic position, below first‚Äêorder predators and possibly close to herbivorous (‚Äòtrophobiotic‚Äô) ants. In examining this hypothesis, we should recognise that the trophic position and specific trophic relations of ants may vary seasonally throughout the period of foraging activity, depending on the changing requirements of the colony and the availability of food (Mooney & Tillberg, 2005; Menke et‚Äâal., 2010; Caut et‚Äâal., 2013).

    We applied stable isotope analysis to study trophic relations among the red wood ant F. aquilonia, myrmecophilous aphids inhabiting trees and herbaceous plants, and invertebrates in the grazing and detrital food chains. The following questions were addressed: (i) what is the trophic position of F. aquilonia; and (ii) to what extent are the seasonal changes in food preferences of ants reflected in their isotopic signatures?

    Materials and methods

    Study site

    The study was conducted in the Novosibirsk region, western Siberia, Russia (N 5500.586, E 8318.460), in a mixed birch forest with pine, spruce, willow, bird cherry, undergrowth of aspen and cover of herbaceous plants. Approximately 500 mounds of F. aquilonia occupied the entire forest territory. Four monodomous ant colonies of similar population size (approximately 106 individuals each) were selected. Neutral zones separated the foraging areas of the studied colonies, which were free of F. aquilonia workers and were from 50 to 200‚Äâm wide; no exchanges of brood, workers or food occurred among these colonies. Foraging areas of the studied colonies were approximately 0.2‚Äâha each, with several dozens of trees visited by foragers. The vegetation cover was similar in all four foraging areas. All the materials were collected within the foraging areas of these four ant colonies.

    Sampling

    To study the trophic position of F. aquilonia, we collected ant foragers and various groups of organisms and substrates representing the primary sources of carbon and nitrogen in the diet of ants: plant litter, aphids and their host plants, aphid honeydew, birch sap and invertebrate prey items of ants. Seasonal variation was assessed by comparing samples collected in spring (18 April to 5 May), summer (7‚Äì27 July) and autumn (24 August to 5 September) (Tables S1 and S2).

    Plant litter

    First, to establish ‚Äòthe isotopic baseline‚Äô, four mixed samples of overwintered birch leaf litter were collected in the foraging area of each ant colony (see later).

    Aphids and plants

    Myrmecophilous aphids tended by F. aquilonia were collected with the parts of their host plants occupied by aphid colonies in summer and autumn. In total, data on 18 pairs of aphid‚Äìplant samples were collected from 93 aphid colonies (Table S1). To identify aphid species, a few specimens from each aphid colony were fixed in 70% alcohol. The aphids were studied on microscope slides prepared using Faure‚ÄêBerlese fluid. The material was deposited at the Institute of Systematics and Ecology of Animals, Siberian Branch of the Russian Academy of Sciences (Novosibirsk). The aphids were identified using Blackman and Eastop (2006). Aphid synonymy follows Favret (2016). A.V. Stekolshchikov verified the aphid determinations (Zoological Institute RAS, St Petersburg, Russia). To avoid the influence of ontogenetic factors on the isotopic signature, larvae and adult aphids were collected in equal proportions.

    Honeydew

    Honeydew was collected in autumn (Table S2) from the four most common species of aphids (Symydobius oblongus (von Heyden), Chaitophorus populeti (Panzer), Chaitophorus cf. ramicola (B√∂rner) and Aphis fabae (Scopoli). Worker ants descending from the aphid colonies with swollen stomachs containing honeydew were carefully removed from the plant, their abdomens gently squeezed with soft plastic tweezers and the drop of honeydew was collected from their mouths using capillaries (Mooney & Tillberg, 2005).

    Birch sap

    Birch sap used by F. aquilonia as the carbohydrate food in early spring was collected on April 26 by drilling small holes in the stems of Betula pendula. Ten‚Äâmillilitres of sap was collected from a single tree in the foraging area of each of the four ant colonies.

    Invertebrate prey items

    In the spring, the activity of F. aquilonia foragers was limited primarily to collecting birch sap and nest‚Äêbuilding materials; therefore, the invertebrate prey of ants was collected only in summer and autumn (S2). Invertebrates or their parts were removed from foragers returning to their mound using soft tweezers (12‚Äì24 prey items per colony). Prey items were collected during the periods of maximal foraging activity of ants (from 10.00 to 12.00‚Äâhours or from 15.00 to 17.00‚Äâhours, depending on the weather). Invertebrate prey items were collected over a total of 8‚Äâh (for 1‚Äâh, once at each colony in summer and autumn).

    Formica aquilonia foragers

    Formica aquilonia foragers were collected in spring, summer and autumn (Table S2). From each colony, five workers were collected on foraging routes, with the same number collected from foraging trees (a total of 30 ants per colony).

    All the samples intended for SIA, except for the prey items, were frozen at ‚àí20‚Äâ¬∞C within 6‚Äâh after collection. Prey items were stored in saturated NaCl solution until identification and were then thoroughly washed with water for 24‚Äâh and dried for SIA.

    Stable isotope analysis

    Sample preparation included drying at 50‚Äâ¬∞C for 2‚Äì3‚Äâdays, weighing (400‚Äì600‚Äâ¬µg for animal and 1500‚Äâ¬µg for plant tissues) and packaging in tin capsules. Plant and litter samples were finely powdered using a ball mill (Retsch MM 200, Haan, Germany). Arthropods (but not aphids) were analysed individually. When possible (for species of Coleoptera, Diptera and Heteroptera), thoraxes and/or head capsules were analysed. Entire small arthropods were analysed. Aphid samples contained five to 20 specimens. In the preparation of worker ants for analysis, abdomens and head capsules were removed and only thoraxes with legs were analysed, which diminished the effects of lipid storage, undigested food in the gut or crop content on isotope measurements (Bl√ºthgen et‚Äâal., 2003; Tillberg et‚Äâal., 2006). SIA was conducted using a Thermo Delta V Plus continuous‚Äêflow isotope ratio mass spectrometer coupled with an elemental analyser (Thermo Flash 1112, Milan, Italy) in the Joint Usage Centre at the Institute of Ecology and Evolution RAS. The isotopic composition of N and C was expressed in the Œ¥‚Äênotation relative to the international standard (atmospheric nitrogen and Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite (VPDB), respectively): Œ¥X(‚Ä∞)‚Äâ=‚Äâ[(Rsample/Rstandard)‚Äâ‚àí‚Äâ1]‚Äâ√ó‚Äâ1000, where R is the ratio of the heavier isotope to the lighter isotope. Samples were analysed with reference gas calibrated against the IAEA reference materials USGS 40 and USGS 41 (glutamic acid). The drift was corrected using internal laboratory standards (glycine, acetanilide). The standard deviations of the Œ¥15N and Œ¥13C values of reference materials (n‚Äâ=‚Äâ8) were <0.2‚Ä∞. With the isotopic analyses, nitrogen and carbon contents (as mass %) were determined in all samples.

    Data analyses

    Local variations in Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of plant samples (leaves, leaf litter) are typically used as a baseline in isotopic studies of litter‚Äêdwelling macroinvertebrates, including ants (Woodcock et‚Äâal., 2012; Korobushkin et‚Äâal., 2014). The stable isotope composition of birch litter did not differ among foraging areas of the studied ant colonies [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C (ranged from ‚àí29.8 to ‚àí29.1‚Ä∞), œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ4.485, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.214; Œ¥15N (ranged from 0.7 to 1.1‚Ä∞), œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ6.463, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.091]; therefore, the data from the four colonies were combined without additional baseline correction.

    The ant prey contained 91 items, of which 16 were fragments of arthropods that could not be identified. The remaining 75 items were identified to species or a higher taxonomic rank. Based on published data on the feeding behaviour of invertebrates, the prey species were tentatively attributed to grazing or detrital food chains (Table S2). To reveal the differences between pairs of data, the Mann‚ÄìWhitney U‚Äêtest was used (with Bonferroni correction in cases of multiple comparisons).     Results

    Plants, aphids and honeydew

    In the investigated community, F. aquilonia maintained trophobiosis with 11 species of myrmecophilous aphids, and foragers of all four ant colonies collected honeydew from all these species. Tree‚Äêdwelling aphids inhabited three species of woody plants (Betula pendula, Populus tremula, and Salix sp.), and grass‚Äêinhabiting aphids were collected on eight species of herbaceous plants (Anthriscus sylvestris, Crepis sibirica, Dactylis glomerata, Filipendula ulmaria, Heracleum sibiricum, Melilotus alba, Tripleurospermum perforatum, and Urtica dioica). The Œ¥13C values of aphids ranged from ‚àí33.6 to ‚àí27.0‚Ä∞, and the Œ¥15N values ranged from ‚àí0.4 to 3.8‚Ä∞ (Fig. 1; Table S1). The Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of the corresponding host plants ranged from ‚àí32.4 to ‚àí27.9‚Ä∞ and from ‚àí1.2 to 2.6‚Ä∞, respectively (Fig. 1; Table S1). Highly significant, positive correlations were found between the isotopic composition of aphids and their host plants (Œ¥13C, œÅ‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.781, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001; Œ¥15N, œÅ‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.688, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.002; Fig. 2).

    The Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of aphids feeding on woody plants (‚àí29.2‚Ä∞ and 2.6‚Ä∞, respectively) were significantly higher than those of aphids feeding on herbaceous plants (‚àí30.9‚Ä∞ and 1.2‚Ä∞, respectively) [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ8.265, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.004; Œ¥15N, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ31.434, P‚Äâ <‚Äâ0.0001; Table S1]. However, no significant differences in isotopic composition were found between woody and herbaceous host plants [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ1.026, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.311; Œ¥15N, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.787, P‚Äâ =‚Äâ0.375; Table S1]. The total nitrogen content in herbaceous plants was two‚Äêfold that of woody plants [2.2% and 1.0%, respectively; Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ13.789, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001].

    Most aphid colonies tended by F. aquilonia belonged to four species: Symydobius oblongus and Chaitophorus populeti, the predominant species from May to September, and Chaitophorus cf. ramicola and Aphis fabae from July to September. The honeydew collected from these four most common aphid species was probably a primary source of carbohydrate for F. aquilonia during the summer.

    The grass‚Äêinhabiting aphid A. fabae colonising the herb Crepis sibirica had Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values significantly lower than those of the three most common tree‚Äêdwelling aphid species [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ13.204, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.004; Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons: U‚Äâ=‚Äâ0, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0083; Table S2; Fig. 3]. Similarly, honeydew produced by A. fabae was depleted in 13C relative to the honeydew of the three tree‚Äêdwelling species [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ18.658, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001]; however, pairwise comparisons between the Œ¥13C value of the honeydew of A. fabae and those of other species revealed no significant differences (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction, P‚Äâ>‚Äâ0.0083; Fig. 3a).

    Because of the very low N content, the Œ¥15N values could be determined for the honeydew of S. oblongus only. The honeydew was depleted in 15N by 3.5‚Ä∞ relative to that of aphids (Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ8.163, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.004) and by 1.6‚Ä∞ relative to that of their host plant (Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ2.922, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.087; Fig. 3b).

    The Œ¥13C value of birch sap was ‚àí26.6‚Ä∞ and was thus enriched in 13C compared with the honeydew of S. oblongus aphids inhabiting birches [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ12.632, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001; Fig. 1]. The sap was also enriched in 15N relative to that of the aphid honeydew [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ6.429, p‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.011; Fig. 1]. The total nitrogen content in the birch sap was much higher than that in the honeydew of S. oblongus [1.5% and 0.1%, respectively; Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ13.714, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001]. The nitrogen content in the honeydew of C. cf. ramicola and A. fabae aphids was approximately 0.1%.

    Trophic position of F. aquilonia

    The isotopic composition of F. aquilonia prey items varied widely and was 7.6‚Ä∞ for Œ¥13C and 13.0‚Ä∞ for Œ¥15N (Fig. 1, Table S2). The prey included predominantly Hemiptera (37%), Diptera (14%) and fragments of arthropods (18%). Approximately 44% of the prey items were in the grazing food chain, 21% in the detrital food chain, and for 35% of prey items, the trophic links could not be identified (Table S2). Animals from the detrital food chain were enriched in 13C in comparison with those from the grazing food chain. Dry‚Äêmass weighted mean values of Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N in the ‚Äògrazing‚Äô, ‚Äòdetrital‚Äô and ‚Äòunknown‚Äô prey groups were ‚àí29.2‚Ä∞, ‚àí26.9‚Ä∞, ‚àí27.1‚Ä∞ and 1.9‚Ä∞, and 6.3‚Ä∞, and 5.1‚Ä∞, respectively. The overall dry‚Äêmass weighted mean Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of prey items were ‚àí28.1‚Ä∞ and 3.9‚Ä∞, respectively.

    The isotopic composition of nitrogen of many phytophagous insects such as Pseudococcidae (3.3‚Ä∞), Cicadellidae (2.6‚Ä∞), Heteroptera (2.0‚Ä∞), and caterpillars of Geometridae (2.2‚Ä∞) were within the range of Œ¥15N values of myrmecophilous aphids (Fig. 1). The nonmyrmecophilous aphid Euceraphis betulae (Koch) (2.3‚Ä∞) was within the same range and formed approximately 23% of the total prey of F. aquilonia. The median Œ¥15N value of all herbivores, including myrmecophilous aphids, was 2.0‚Ä∞.

    The isotopic composition of F. aquilonia foragers differed significantly among the four colonies [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ39.728, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001; Œ¥15N, œá2(3)‚Äâ=‚Äâ40.997, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001] and ranged in Œ¥13C from ‚àí26.4 to ‚àí25.7‚Ä∞ and in Œ¥15N from 5.3 to 5.8‚Ä∞ (Table S2). The median Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia from all colonies and sampling events were the highest among Œ¥13C values for all invertebrates in our study (‚àí26.0‚Ä∞; Fig. 1). Foragers were enriched in 13C by 2.1‚Ä∞ relative to the dry‚Äêmass weighted average Œ¥13C value of invertebrate prey, by 0.6‚Ä∞ relative to the birch sap and by 1.1‚Äì2.7‚Ä∞ relative to the honeydew of aphids feeding on woody plants.

    The median Œ¥15N value of red wood ants (5.5‚Ä∞) was 3.5‚Ä∞ higher than that of herbivores, including aphids, and 4.9‚Ä∞ higher than that in plants. Formica aquilonia had a higher Œ¥15N value relative to that of litter‚Äêdwelling Linyphiidae spiders (4.4‚Ä∞) but lower than the Œ¥15N value of predatory Geophilidae centipedes (6.7‚Ä∞).

    Seasonal changes in the diet of F. aquilonia

    The stable isotope composition of worker ants differed significantly depending on the sampling time [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(2)‚Äâ=‚Äâ14.390, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.001; Œ¥15N, œá2(2)‚Äâ=‚Äâ7.382, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.025). The Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of worker ants were almost equal in spring (‚àí25.9‚Ä∞ and 5.5‚Ä∞, respectively) and summer (‚àí25.9‚Ä∞ and 5.6‚Ä∞, respectively) (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction: Œ¥13C, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ776, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.817; Œ¥15N, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ798, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.985). From summer to autumn, the Œ¥13C and Œ¥15N values of worker ants decreased significantly, by 0.3‚Ä∞ (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction: Œ¥13C, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ415, P‚Äâ<‚Äâ0.0001) and 0.2‚Ä∞ (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction: Œ¥15N, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ552, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.017), respectively (Fig. 4). From spring to autumn, isotopic signatures of worker ants decreased significantly, by 0.3‚Ä∞ (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction: Œ¥13C, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ504, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.004) and 0.1‚Ä∞ (Mann‚ÄìWhitney test with Bonferroni correction: Œ¥15N, U‚Äâ=‚Äâ560, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.021), respectively.

    The time of sampling did not significantly affect the species composition of the prey of F. aquilonia, as indicated by a high Bray‚ÄìCurtis similarity value (0.735) between the prey collected in summer and autumn. Additionally, no significant differences in isotopic signatures of prey samples were found between summer and autumn [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ2.224, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.136; Œ¥15N, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.026, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.871; Fig. 4]. Isotopic signatures of plants also did not change from summer to autumn [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: Œ¥13C, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.989, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.320; Œ¥15N, œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.156, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.193; Fig. 4]. The median Œ¥13C value of myrmecophilous aphids was similar in summer and autumn samples [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ3.108, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.078], whereas the median Œ¥15N values of aphids decreased by 0.1‚Ä∞ [Kruskal‚ÄìWallis test: œá2(1)‚Äâ=‚Äâ4.457, P‚Äâ=‚Äâ0.035; Fig. 4].

    Discussion

    Plants, aphids and honeydew

    The Œ¥13C values of aphids did not differ significantly from those of the host plants (Figs 1 and 2), similar to earlier observations (Spence & Rosenheim, 2005). By contrast, the honeydew produced by aphids was highly enriched in 13C relative to the aphids and their host plants (Fig. 3). A similar 13C enrichment of honeydew is reported in several other studies (Yoneyama et‚Äâal., 1997; Mooney & Tillberg, 2005; Sagers & Goggin, 2007), which may be related to the high Œ¥13C values of sugars in aphid excretions and to the low Œ¥13C values of the fat reserves in aphids (Gearing, 1991).

    Tree‚Äêdwelling aphid species (S. oblongus, C. populeti, and C. cf. ramicola) had higher Œ¥13C values than the species inhabiting the herb layer (A. fabae). Most probably, this difference was a reflection of a well known ‚Äòcanopy effect‚Äô, i.e. the depletion of the ground vegetation in 13C relative to canopy plants (van der Merwe & Medina, 1991; Brooks et‚Äâal., 1997). The pattern of Œ¥13C values in honeydew (Figs 1 and 3) suggested that high Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia reflected a strong trophic dependence on tree‚Äêdwelling aphids, whereas aphid species colonising herbaceous plants were of much less importance to ants. Because of the high variability in the 13C content of aphid honeydew, SIA could be used to study the vertical spatial organisation of honeydew foraging in ants (with canopy layers preferred for foraging, at least for honeydew collection).

    Tree‚Äêdwelling aphids were enriched in 15N relative to grass‚Äêinhabiting species, although the enrichment of host plants was not different. Therefore, to explain the difference between tree‚Äêdwelling and grass‚Äêinhabiting aphids, the trophic fractionation of 15N must have been somewhat higher in the aphids feeding on trees. This could be related to the differences in total N content in tissues of herbs and trees (2% and 1%, respectively), as trophic fractionation of 15N in aphids is known to be affected by the dietary quality of phloem sap (Sagers & Goggin, 2007). The Œ¥15N value in the honeydew of S. oblongus aphids feeding on birch was approximately 4‚Ä∞ lower than the Œ¥15N of aphid tissues, and the reasons for this depletion remain unclear.

    Trophic position of F. aquilonia

    According to earlier reports, depending on the forest type, the prey of red wood ants consists primarily of Lepidoptera, including larvae of Geometridae (Horstmann, 1974; Skinner, 1980; Punttila et‚Äâal., 2004) and Diptera (Rosengren & Sundstr√∂m, 1991; Domisch et‚Äâal., 2009). In our study, the composition of prey of F. aquilonia displayed a wide taxonomic and trophic variety that is typical of generalist predators, with a predominance of phytophagous insects, mostly aphids.

    Variation in Œ¥15N values among the four colonies of F. aquilonia (0.5‚Ä∞) was lower than the intercolony variation in Œ¥15N values for five ant species (2‚Äì6‚Ä∞; Tillberg et‚Äâal., 2006), which might be an indication of habitat similarity of the studied colonies.

    The median Œ¥15N value of F. aquilonia exceeded the median Œ¥15N values of aphids and other herbivorous insects by approximately 3.5‚Ä∞, which roughly corresponds to the level of trophic fractionation of 15N/14N in other ants (3.0‚Ä∞; Feldhaar et‚Äâal., 2010) and other invertebrates (3.3‚Ä∞; McCutchan et‚Äâal., 2003). Thus, in contrast to our expectations, these data indicated that F. aquilonia occupied the position of first‚Äêorder predator in the studied community. The trophic position as a predator distinguished F. aquilonia from the combined data on eight species of the genus Formica (Fiedler et‚Äâal., 2007) and placed them closer to Formica podzolica, which is similarly enriched in 15N relative to aphids, by 3.3‚Ä∞ (Mooney & Tillberg, 2005). The close trophic positions of these two species can be explained by their ecological similarities because, similar to red wood ants, F. podzolica occurs in relatively large colonies (from 103 to 104 workers, up to 105 individuals), forages in the same layers of vegetation and the diet includes arthropods and sugar‚Äêrich liquids (Savolainen et‚Äâal., 1996; Mooney & Tillberg, 2005).

    The Œ¥15N values of F. aquilonia were intermediate between litter‚Äêdwelling (linyphiid spiders) and soil‚Äêdwelling (geophilid centipedes) predators (Fig. 1). However, based on direct observations, F. aquilonia participated in the grazing food chain, with at least 40% of the total prey items being above‚Äêground herbivores. Thus, red wood ants feed actively on both types of prey, linking detritus‚Äêbased and plant‚Äêbased energy pathways in the food web.

    As noted previously, red wood ants are obligate trophobiotic species that obtain carbon primarily from aphid honeydew (Horstmann, 1974; Domisch et‚Äâal., 2009). Therefore, the high Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia were probably primarily related to feeding on the 13C‚Äêenriched honeydew of tree‚Äêdwelling aphids. However, because litter‚Äêdwelling arthropods are also consistently enriched in 13C (‚àº3‚Äì4‚Ä∞) relative to plant litter due to the increase in 13C content in saprotrophic microorganisms (Hyodo et‚Äâal., 2010; Potapov et‚Äâal., 2013), the high Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia also suggested trophic links with the detrital food chain. Indeed, the Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia (‚àí26.0‚Ä∞) were similar to those of invertebrates from the detrital food chain (‚àí26.9‚Ä∞).

    To summarise, based on our analysis, the hypothesis that red wood ants occupied a low trophic position in the invertebrate community was rejected. Despite the major role of honeydew in F. aquilonia nutrition (Domisch et‚Äâal., 2009), we found this species was a first‚Äêorder predator.

    Seasonal changes in the diet of F. aquilonia

    In red wood ants, the seasonal changes in the diet from spring to autumn included a reduction in the number of prey consumed, switching from tree sap collection in the spring to collection of aphid honeydew, and an increase in the volume of carbohydrate food required to feed adult workers (Dlussky, 1967; Horstmann, 1972; Skinner, 1980).

    The Œ¥13C value of F. aquilonia decreased significantly from spring and summer to autumn (Fig. 4a). Because the carbon isotopic composition of plants, aphids and prey of ants in summer and autumn was similar, the seasonal variations detected in the carbon isotopic signatures of foragers could be associated primarily with changes in the consumption of liquid food substrates. The literature does not contain data on the time required for the assimilation of isotopes into ant tissues, and the extent to which carbon and nitrogen isotopic signatures of worker ants reflect their recent diet as adults or their diet as larvae is also unknown. In F. aquilonia, the development from an egg to a worker requires approximately 3‚Äì4‚Äâweeks, and 5‚Äì6 more weeks are required to become a forager (Otto, 1958). Hence, we suggest that 8‚Äì10‚Äâweeks are required to change the isotopic signatures of F. aquilonia foragers. Therefore, the 0.3‚Ä∞ decrease in Œ¥13C values in F. aquilonia foragers from the end of April and mid‚ÄêJuly to early September apparently reflected changes in the ant diet, which proceeded from consuming birch sap with high Œ¥13C (‚àí26.6‚Ä∞) in April and honeydew of the most common tree‚Äêdwelling aphids (Œ¥13C from ‚àí28.7 to ‚àí27.1‚Ä∞) in June‚ÄìJuly to a mix that included honeydew of grass‚Äêinhabiting aphids (Œ¥13C, ‚àí31.4‚Ä∞), which became much more abundant in the second half of the summer. Platner et‚Äâal. (2012) reported a seasonal decrease in Œ¥13C values of F. rufibarbis and Lasius grandis ants in citrus plantations due to the seasonal decrease in Œ¥13C of citrus trees, which provided the primary source of food for these species, rather than to changes in the composition of food substrates.

    From spring and summer to autumn, the decrease in Œ¥15N in F. aquilonia was approximately 0.1‚Äì0.2‚Ä∞. A similar seasonal decline in Œ¥15N values is recorded in F. podzolica workers, which was explained by an increase in the proportion of herbivore prey from early to late summer (Mooney & Tillberg, 2005). The autumnal decline in the Œ¥15N values of F. aquilonia foragers might reflect changes in their larval diet but this is difficult to determine. We did not find any significant differences in Œ¥15N values of plants or prey of ants or in the composition of prey between summer and autumn. The data on the composition of F. aquilonia prey were insufficient to answer the question whether the temporal change in Œ¥15N values was associated with a change in the trophic position of ants. In the second half of the summer, foragers probably obtained a higher proportion of nitrogen from aphid honeydew with relatively low Œ¥15N values compared with birch sap in the spring. The decrease in the Œ¥15N value of myrmecophilous aphids from the summer to autumn could also explain this decline in Œ¥15N values of foragers. Other studies also suggest that abundant feeding on honeydew and nectar leads to low Œ¥15N values in trophobiotic ant species (Bl√ºthgen et‚Äâal., 2003; Davidson et‚Äâal., 2003; Menke et‚Äâal., 2010).

    Conclusions

    Based on the comparison of Œ¥15N values of F. aquilonia, its prey and myrmecophilous aphids, this species occupied the trophic position of a first‚Äêorder predator. Despite obligate trophobiotic relationships with aphids, the Œ¥15N values of F. aquilonia exceeded those of herbivores and aphids by approximately 3.5‚Ä∞ in the studied community.

    The red wood ant F. aquilonia had higher Œ¥13C values than the members of the grazing food chain, which was explained by the consumption of 13C‚Äêenriched aphid honeydew. Tree‚Äêdwelling aphids tended by F. aquilonia were enriched in 13C and 15N relative to aphid species inhabiting herbaceous plants. Additionally, honeydew Œ¥13C values in tree‚Äêdwelling aphids were higher than those for grass‚Äêinhabiting species. Thus, the high Œ¥13C value of F. aquilonia showed that tree‚Äêdwelling aphids were more important for red wood ants than were grass‚Äêinhabiting aphid species. The decrease in Œ¥13C values of F. aquilonia during the period of foraging activity was attributed to the transition from the collection of tree sap and feeding on aphid honeydew from woody plants with high 13C content in the spring and early summer to a more diverse liquid diet in late summer, which included 13C‚Äêdepleted honeydew of aphids from herbaceous plants. The prevalence of 15N‚Äêdepleted aphid honeydew in the ant diet during the second half of the summer is discussed as one possible explanation for the seasonal decline in Œ¥15N values of the worker ants.
