    Abstract

    The timing and duration of sexual brood production in ants can affect their rate of spread and colony growth. Because protein is key to larval growth, queen survival and fecundity, macronutrient collection by foraging workers is expected to favour protein prior to and throughout gyne production. However, food preference driven by the production of gynes may be overridden by a preference associated with worker production, especially if investment in workers vastly outweighs that of sexual brood and workers are produced on different schedules. Food preferences alone may not indicate the availability of that food type in the environment. On Christmas Island, Indian Ocean, sexual brood of the invasive Yellow Crazy Ant, Anoplolepis gracilipes (Smith, 1857), was produced annually during a single period associated with the onset of the wet season. However, workers were produced continuously throughout the year and colony investment to worker production measured by the standing biomass of eggs, larvae and pupae, typically exceeded 98%. High, aseasonal investment in worker production, together with aseasonal worker activity, would suggest that there should be no seasonal preference shown by workers at food stations containing both protein and carbohydrate. However, workers showed a preference for one food type over another on 46 of 61 occasions at one site and on 31 of 41 occasions at another. When a preference was shown, it was predominantly for proteinrich food during the dry season and almost always for carbohydrate at the onset and during the wet season. We suggest that these preferences reflect seasonal shortages in key resources when invertebrates (proteinrich) are scarce during the dry season and honeydew from scale insects (carbohydrate) is depleted by rain during the wet season. On Christmas Island, timing and duration of the dry season preference for protein has been exploited by the control program for A.gracilipes supercolonies, which deploys a toxin in a proteinaceous bait matrix during dry periods.

    Introduction

    Life histories and mating patterns of ants can provide insight into their phenomenal ecological success (Hlldobler & Wilson 1990; Peeters & Molet 2010). The timing and duration of sexual brood production can have significant implications for colony growth, dispersal of queens, colonyfounding strategies and rate of colony spread (Hlldobler & Wilson 1990). Founding queen ants typically require large metabolic reserves to sustain ovary development, egg production and larval growth at their new nest site (Brian 1973; Hlldobler & Wilson 1990; Nation 2001) and are dependent upon workers to retrieve these food items. It is generally nitrogen in the form of amino acids and proteins that is the limiting resource for colony growth and reproduction at this time (Tobin 1995; Davidson 1997; Nation 2001) and that it is larvae present in the colony that increases the proportion of protein in their diet (Dussutour & Simpson 2009). However, there is increasing evidence that carbohydraterich honeydew from hemipteran mutualists can have greater benefits in terms of colony growth than proteinaceous insect prey (Shik & Silverman 2013).

    The food choices made by worker ants are driven by their individual nutritional needs, and information given to them by colony members about the requirements of conspecifics in the nest (Cook etal. 2010) and reproductive status of the colony (Stein etal. 1990). Workers choose carbohydrates to fuel their own activity and collect proteinaceous foods to either pass onto developing larvae, the queen, other workers or to hoard for extraction when necessary (Cook etal. 2010). Recruitment to food sources depends not only on the rate of information transfer between workers and on recruitment strategy (Cassill 2003) but also on the quality, stability and perceived quantity of the resource (Sundstrom 1993; Kay 2002). Interpreting patterns of recruitment to different food choices is complicated by other factors such as division of labour, overlapping generations and foraging costs (Cook etal. 2010). The relative availability of complementary resources in the environment can also affect the feeding preferences of workers (Kay 2003), perhaps more so than nutritional needs of the colony. There can also be temporal variation in the resource requirements of ant colonies; both temperate and tropical ant species have cyclical patterns of brood production (Stein etal. 1990; Noordijk etal. 2008; Hart & Tschinkel 2012), and the onset of a wet or dry season can provide strong environmental cues for patterns of food collection and recruitment. However, little is known about how the interaction between seasonality and brood production affects food choices by ants.

    Traditionally, the study of ant reproductive phenology has emphasised periods during which sexuals are produced and the resource demands that places on the colony as a whole and the foraging preferences of individual workers. There are older studies that have linked periods during which brood (including sexuals) are produced to a notable preference for protein among foraging workers (e.g. Sorensen etal. 1983; Stein etal. 1990). However, these studies have not focused on the timing of the production of gynes alone and linked any shift in seasonal food preference with temperature rather than with any biotic variables. Food preference of S.invicta has also been labelled idiosyncratic (Glunn etal. 1981). In ant species that form highdensity supercolonies where the ratio of gynes to workers is generally low, colony food preference may be driven more by the nutritional requirements of the workers and their production schedules than by queens. In these species, the workers' need for energy may drive recruitment to, and collection of, food types high in sugars. Carbohydrates in the form of excretory honeydew from scale insects are an important dietary component for species of invasive ants that typically have high ratios of workers to gynes (Heller 2004; Abbott 2005; Helms 2013). Indeed, the five species of invasive ants whose impacts are considered the most damaging have disproportionately high numbers of workers; these species are also associated with honeydewproducing insects in their introduced range (Helms 2013).

    The invasive yellow crazy ant, Anoplolepis gracilipes, has become a widespread pest in tropical and subtropical locations worldwide (Wetterer 2005). On Christmas Island (Indian Ocean), this species forms a mosaic of high and lowdensity supercolonies (O'Dowd etal. 2003; Abbott 2005; Thomas etal. 2010), where it eliminates endemic red land crabs (a keystone species: Gecarcoidea natalis) causing rapid changes in the composition and structure of rainforest (O'Dowd etal. 2003). The density of worker ants in supercolonies can reach 2254 foraging ants/m2 (Abbott 2005), and comparative (O'Dowd etal. 2003, K Abbott unpubl. data 2002) and experimental studies (S Wittman etal. unpubl. data 2012) indicate that these extraordinary densities are facilitated by mutualistic associations with honeydewproducing scale insects, mostly in the canopy of rainforest trees. Most of the proteinaceous intake in supercolonies is in the form of small litter invertebrates, but the annual breeding migration of red land crabs (Hicks 1985) can provide the ants with a pulse of proteinrich food at the onset of the wet season if the crabs migrate through supercolonies that lie in their path (O'Dowd etal. 2003; Green & O'Dowd 2009; Green etal. 2011).

    An understanding of the reproductive phenology and food preference of A.gracilipes may help predict population escalation and supercolony spread. Moreover, in areas where A.gracilipes has become a serious invader (Lewis etal. 1976; Lester & Tavite 2004; Abbott etal. 2007; Green & O'Dowd 2009; Hoffmann & Saul 2010; Savage etal. 2011), life history knowledge can benefit conservation management by highlighting when ant colonies prefer, or at least recruit, to and collect a specific food type. The timing of a particular food preference by foraging A.gracilipes can help managers time the application of a corresponding bait matrix for maximum uptake by workers and prevent the production of sexual brood.

    Here, we first describe the reproductive phenology of A.gracilipes in highdensity supercolonies on Christmas Island, with particular emphasis on the timing and duration of sexual vs. worker brood production. Second, we quantify over time the relative investment of colony resources in the production of workers compared with reproductive brood to determine which is more likely to drive temporal variation in feeding preferences by the workers of this species. Third, we document forager recruitment to carbohydrate and proteinrich foods, and evaluate these patterns as a potential indicator of colony macronutrient requirements vs. environmental availability.

    Methods and Materials

    Study sites

    Christmas Island is an elevated oceanic limestone island that lies 360km south of Java in the northeastern Indian Ocean (10540E, 1030S). This elevated (to 364m) small island (135km2) experiences a monsoonal climate in which most of the 2000mm mean annual rainfall occurs between December and May (Falkland 1986). Approximately 74% of the island is still covered with floristically simple rainforest; tall evergreen rainforest occurs on deep soils in the higher regions of the island, while shorter, more deciduous forest occurs on shallower soil on fringing terraces. Common tree species include Barringtonia racemosa, Celtis timorensis, Cryptocarya nitens, Hernandia ovigera, Inocarpus fagifer, Pisonia umbellifera, Planchonella nitens and Syzygium nervosum.

    This study was conducted in 20002002, when supercolonies of A.gracilipes were widespread across the island (Abbott 2006; Green & O'Dowd 2009). Thirtyfour supercolonies varying in spatial extent from less than 1ha to more than 500ha occupied a total area of more than 2500ha in September 2002 (Fig.1). Reproductive phenology was studied at six supercolony sites, all characterised by extremely high densities of A.gracilipes workers foraging on the forest floor (>800ants/m2, Abbott 2005), the absence of red land crabs, widespread seedling recruitment and a relatively thick layer of leaf litter.

    Reproductive phenology

    We monitored brood production of A.gracilipes at these six sites from December 2000 to December 2002 (Fig.1). Yellow crazy ants readily colonise artificial domiciles that can be harvested from the field for observation of colony composition representative of the more fragmented naturally occurring colonies in the surrounding environment (Van der Goot 1916; Baker 1976). In each plot, 60 black polyethylene pipes (polypipes; 200mm long, 40mm diameter) were placed on the ground at randomly selected locations on 50m50m grid with intersections at 5m intervals. The polypipes were capped at one end, and small amounts of leaf litter and soil were placed inside to facilitate colonisation by YCA. After 2 months, we commenced collecting five tubes per month from each plot (total 30 tubes each month) to monitor reproduction. Often, there were fewer than five tubes colonised by A.gracilipes. We collected up to five colonised tubes at each site and replaced them so that artificial nest density remained constant. Occupied tubes were selected at random, collected into plastic bags and then frozen until processing. Nine life stages were counted: egg masses, queen larvae, other larvae, queen pupae, other pupae, workers, males, dealate queens and alate queens. The mean number of individuals in each life stage was calculated using site averages (up to n=5 pipes/site) as replicates. Rainfall was recorded at Christmas Island airport at 261m ASL.

    Investment of colony resources: workers vs. reproductives

    Colony foraging dynamics may be dictated by temporal variation in investment of resources to the most abundant castes. Abundance data were converted to standing biomass using stagespecific masses derived from both this study and from Baker (1976) (Table1). At each sampling date, we used the sum of eggs, worker larvae and worker pupae biomass as a proxy for colony investment to workers, and the sum of queen larvae and queen pupae biomass as a proxy for colony investment in gynes. We did not include male biomass as their presence did not imply that it was their natal nest and their contribution to biomass was miniscule.

    Food preference

    We used food stations to monitor seasonal changes in recruitment to carbohydrate or proteinrich sources. A food station consisted of an elevated plastic feeding platform (10cm8cm) supported 8cm above the ground on a steel peg onto which approximately 5g of both proteinrich (Black & Gold tuna/sardine cat food) and carbohydratebased (fruit conserve) were placed at each end of the platform. A narrow plastic ramp at the middle of one edge connected the platform to the ground, and we ensured that this was only point of access by placing a sticky band of Tanglefoot on the peg beneath the platform. This design forced worker ants ascending the ramp to make a choice between food types. Cat food is commonly used in field experiments or collections of ants as a proteinrich source (Agosti etal. 2000). Five food stations per site were used on each sampling date (occasionally four stations if one malfunctioned), and the number of ants at each food type was recorded after 30min. Food preference was monitored weekly at two sites initially (ECR and WAD), and a third (WBT) when the supercolony at WAD collapsed (see Abbott 2005). At ECR (Fig.1), observations were made on 61 occasions between February 2001 and November 2002; at WAD, observations were matched with ECR until June 2002. Observations at WBT were from August to November 2002. Rain reduced the numbers of ants that were present on the platforms, and during heavy rains, either baits were washed off the stations or ants did not recruit to the baits at all. Data were not recorded on these occasions.

    Although decisions by ants of what type of food to collect and when to collect it are driven by both internal and external factors (Cook etal. 2010 and references therein), for analysis, we considered the presence of a worker ant at one or the other food type an independent sampling event. If workers show no choice, the null expectation would be an even split of workers visiting the two food types, i.e. a proportion of 0.5 at both. We used the normal approximation to the binomial distribution to test for significant departures from this proportion and regarded such departures as evidence of preference for one food type or the other. Critical values of pE, the expected proportion of all ants on the platforms recorded at the proteinaceous food source (0.5), were calculated as 0.5E, where E=Z/(2n). Here, n is the total number of ants at both food types, and Z takes a value determined by central tendency theory to reflect a given confidence level. We used Z=1.9599 (i.e. almost two standard deviations), providing 95% confidence intervals of pE against which to compare the observed proportion of ants at the protein food source, pO. If pO fell between 0.5E and 0.5+E, then we concluded the proportion of ants at protein was not significantly different to the null expectation and concluded that ants showed no preference. If pO>0.5+E, we concluded the ants showed a preference for protein, and if pO<0.5E, we concluded a preference for carbohydrate. Both n and pO were calculated from ant counts pooled across four or five platforms on each sampling date. The few sampling dates on which n<40 were not included in analyses.

    Results

    Reproductive phenology

    Egg masses, workers, worker larvae and worker pupae of A.gracilipes were present in artificial nests all year round, and adult workers consistently made up over 50% of nest contents despite their numbers probably being underestimated in the tubes because most workers would likely be out foraging (Fig.2ad). A.gracilipes workers were often found in the tubes with only worker larvae and pupae, especially during the dry season. Pupae were also found piled up in hollows of litter or on leaves on the ground, where workers moved them around continuously.

    Sexual brood were produced annually, commencing just prior to the onset of the wet season (Fig.2eh). At all sites and in both years, we detected males in polypipes commencing in October, with a peak of 3512 males per tube (range 370, n=6) in 2001 and 159 per tube (range 042, n= 5) at one site only in 2002. The production of males was followed by the presence of queen larvae, queen pupae, then dealate (unwinged) queens. There was a conspicuous absence of a peak of alate (winged) queen production in tubes after queen larvae and pupae were produced. Although dealate queens were found in all nests throughout the year, their numbers peaked at the end of the 2002 wet season (Fig.2h; mean 1716 queens per tube; range 098, n =6). Males were not seen in any supercolonies from March through to August (except one male at site 1 in April 2002). Single alate queens were found infrequently in tubes throughout the year, but it was not determined if these queens had newly emerged or were producing brood, or had been inseminated at all.

    Investment of colony resources: workers vs. reproductives

    In supercolonies, A.gracilipes consistently directed most colony resources to the production of workers. The mean biomass of worker eggs, larvae and pupae varied aseasonally throughout the study, between 427mg/pipe/site (meanSE) and 44790mg/pipe/site (Fig.2e). Queen larvae and queen pupae were completely absent in pipes for half the surveys, and when present, their mean biomass varied between a 0.030.03 and 3625mg/pipe/site. Using biomasses summed across multiple monthly sampling at each site, most resources were invested in the production of workers (98% at ECR, 99% at JAM, 99% at PUM, 99% at WAD, 93% at WBT and 99% at WCR; see Fig.1 for site numbers).

    Food preference

    On most sampling occasions foraging workers of A.gracilipes rapidly recruited to food stations in large numbers. At ECR, the median number of ants per food station was 91 after 30min (range 0228, n=281 counts pooled across dates); at WAD, it was 61 (range 0201, n=188); and at WBT, it was 22 (range 0131, n=55).

    Foraging workers at ECR showed distinct patterns of food preference that were broadly aligned with the wet and dry seasons (Fig.3a). The commencement of the 2001 dry season in April marked the start of a period lasting until the start of October when ants either foraged without preference (8 of 22 surveys), or more commonly (14 of 22 surveys), showed a preference for proteinaceous food. However, the preference for protein was usually modest and rarely exceeded 2:1. Foragers did not show a preference again until late November 2001, at which time they switched to a preference for carbohydrate in all wet season surveys (11/11 surveys) to midApril 2002. Again, the strength of the preference was usually modest but occasionally exceeded 2:1. The wet season finished in April 2002, and in almost all (18/20) dry season surveys from midJune to late November 2002 foraging ants again showed a significant preference for protein, which was particularly strong (3:1) in late September to early November.

    Broadly similar patterns were observed at WAD and WBT (Fig.3b). During the dry season of 2001 (mid April to December), foraging ants showed an even ratio of no preference (8/26 surveys), preference for carbohydrate (9/26 surveys) or preference for protein (9/26 surveys) but not in any obvious temporal pattern. However, during the following wet season (JanuaryApril 2002) foragers showed a preference for carbohydrate in all surveys (8/8 surveys). Just as at ECR, in most surveys where preference was shown, it was relatively modest, but the extreme preference for carbohydrate in the last four surveys at WAD just before the supercolony declined completely (8:1 in late March, 31:1 in midApril, 57:1 in late April and 56:1 in early June 2002) is notable. Dry season sampling was continued at WBT from midSeptember through the end of November 2002, during which time foragers showed a food preference in 9 of 11 surveys, 8 of which were for protein.

    Discussion

    Phenology

    The reproductive phenology of A.gracilipes on Christmas Island is very similar to patterns observed at other sites with highly seasonal rainfall, including Indonesia (Van der Goot 1916), Solomon Islands (Greenslade 1971a,b), Papua New Guinea (Baker 1976), Seychelles (Haines & Haines 1978) and India (Rao & Veeresh 1991). At all these locations, the production of sexual brood coincides with the transition from the dry to the wet season (see Fig.4 for details). On Christmas Island, males were first detected in artificial nests in October, just before the first rains of the wet season reached peak abundance in November and December, and were largely absent by January. Very few alate queens were found in artificial nests, but half of these were recorded in November and December, coincident with males. Alate males were also recorded in canopy malaise traps at WCR and WBT on 29 January 2001 and at ECR on 8 February 2001 (PT Green & DJ O'Dowd unpubl. data 2004), later than indicated by the artificial nest data. In the following wet season, we recorded alate queens and winged males together in canopy malaise traps at the WBT site over the week leading up to 25 January 2002, almost 2 months after the wet season began on 2 December 2001 (Abbott 2006). These observations suggest the occurrence of mating flights over the duration of the wet season. However, mating flights have not been recorded in this species, and the dissections of ovaries from these collections have been inconclusive as to the timing of mating (C Vanderwoude pers. comm. 2005). In contrast with the highly seasonal nature of sexual brood production, egg masses, worker larvae and pupae were present in artificial nests yearround with no obvious seasonal peaks. This is consistent with the aseasonal production schedule for workers found by Haines and Haines (1978) in the Seychelles but differs to the obviously seasonal production of workers in New Guinea (Baker 1976) and the Solomon Islands (Greenslade 1971b) during the rainy season.

    The wet season on Christmas Island usually occurs from December through to May, but during this study, the wet seasons in both 2000/01 and 2001/02 commenced in January and finished by April. Observations in the Seychelles suggest that most natural nests typically contained between 27 and 56 queens (based on 95% CI; Haines & Haines 1978). On Christmas Island, we observed much lower numbers in the artificial polypipe nests (Fig.1), and implicit in this study is the assumption that colonies in artificial domiciles are representative of the more fragmented natural colonies (Van der Goot 1916). The only component not accurately represented at the time of sampling was the total number of workers associated with each nest (Baker 1976). But in natural nests, queens were more abundant (K Abbott pers. obs. 2002), and occasionally, we found more than 1000 queens in single nests; supernests appeared to be relatively common in A.gracilipes supercolonies in and under large dead tree trunks and logs on the ground.

    A single sexual brood production event has significant implications for the rate of spread of A.gracilipes. Overcrowding of queens in a nest might cause inseminated queens to leave the nest in polygynous colonies (Hlldobler & Wilson 1990). If this is the case, an increase in the number of queens produced in one nest could increase the rate of movement of queens out of the nest and increase the rate of boundary expansion of supercolonies. On Christmas Island, however, such a boundary expansion was not observed. During the period from November through March, supercolony boundaries were either static or contracted (2001 and 2002, respectively; Abbott 2006). The most rapid rate of supercolony expansion was during the dry season when sexual brood was not produced, indicating that overcrowding of nests might not be a reason for an inseminated queen to leave the nest. Rather, it is more likely that at times of peak forager activity, they accompany inseminated queens to new nest sites in areas of increased food resources, namely honeydew from canopydwelling scale insects (Abbott & Green 2007). Alternatively, in the wet season when protein is abundant, there is little need for foragers to travel in order to fulfil their protein requirements, so queens simply find a new nest site close by. In the dry season foragers must travel greater distance to discover protein sources, thereby expanding boundaries, taking queens with them and forming new colonies outside the original supercolony.

    Colony investment and food preference

    At high densities in supercolonies, A.gracilipes invests a large proportion of colony resources to worker brood relative to queens. We used the sum of egg masses, worker larvae and worker pupae as a proxy for colony investment to worker production, and the biomass of queen larvae and pupae as the same proxy for investment in queens. Differences in the developmental times of workers and queens, and the omission of males from these calculations, make these proxies an imperfect estimate of colony investment in workers vs. gynes, but they are likely to be indicative of where most colony resources are invested. By biomass, we estimated that at five of six sites, 98% or more of the biomass of immature stages was in future worker ants. Similar calculations using data in table2 of Baker (1976) yield 93% at his rainforest site in New Guinea.

    On Christmas Island, there is no obvious reason to expect a seasonal shift in food preferences to cater for sexual brood production. The density of workers in supercolonies is consistently and exceptionally high yearround (Abbott 2005) supported by the continuous production of worker brood (this study). Given this, there must be a consistently high demand for both carbohydrate resources to sustain foragers and enhance colony growth (Wilder etal. 2011), and proteinrich resources to sustain larvae and worker brood production by queens. Any increased demand for protein coincident with sexual brood production during the wet season would be a tiny fraction of colony demand and would not be detectable against the demands associated with worker brood production. The marked seasonality in feeding preferences for complementary resources shown by A.gracilipes in supercolonies is therefore remarkable and could be driven by relative availability in the environment (Kay 2003) rather than by colony dynamics per se.

    This view is supported by the low abundance of litter invertebrates during the dry season on Christmas Island (Green etal. 1999), similar to patterns in other seasonal forests in the neotropics (Levings & Windsor 1982) and in Australia (Frith & Frith 1990). Although standlevel production of honeydew in YCA supercolonies has not been measured on Christmas Island, it is likely to be lowest during the wet season; the abundance of adult honeydewproducing scale insects is lowest at this time (G Neumann unpubl. data 2011). Even if standlevel honeydew production is aseasonal, its availability to foraging YCA workers could still be highly seasonal because heavy rain during the wet season would wash much of it away.

    It is likely that these seasonal shortages in protein and carbohydrate explain most of the temporal variation in food preferences by A.gracilipes workers during the study. In any case, the shift away from proteinaceous food occurred just when queens began producing sexual brood just before the start of the wet season. When considered in this way, the greaterthanexpected recruitment to food types in this study may not be a preference as such but a measure of foraging effort necessary to gain the required amount of protein and carbohydrate to maintain optimal colony functioning (Dussutour & Simpson 2008, 2012; Cook etal. 2011). This is a more qualitative behavioural trait that has been used to assess the availability of resources to ants (Kay 2002).

    The extreme preference shown by workers of A.gracilipes for carbohydratebased food resources at WAD, but not at ECR, during the latter part of the 2001/02, wet season is noteworthy and might aid in future investigation of nutrient regulation strategies in booming and busting populations of ants. At most times when there was a preference for carbohydrate, the ratio of workers at complementary food resources was around 2:1 to 4:1, but during MarchMay 2001, the ratio reached 57:1. This coincided with the collapse of the supercolony at WAD, with ant density declining almost to nil by May (Abbott 2005). In all likelihood, supercolony collapse was driven by a precipitous decline in the density of honeydewproducing scale insects, which created an extreme scarcity of carbohydrate resources and led to colony decline (Wilder etal. 2011).

    The seasonal shift in recruitment to food types has not been wellstudied in other locations where A.gracilipes forms supercolonies. Similar to this study, Haines and Haines (1978) found that most colony investment in the Seychelles was directed to the production of workers (75% by biomass), and that because adult and brood were produced throughout the year leading, they thought there should be aseasonality in food requirements. In the Solomon Islands, Greenslade (1971b) noted that occupancy by A.gracilipes in coconut palms harbouring honeydew producers fell during the wet season, which he interpreted as a shift to searching for prey in the ground layer as it became more abundant under wet conditions. In New Guinea, Baker (1976) was unable to determine if the wet season peak in sexual brood production was associated with a change in food preference or changes in forager efficiency or intensity.

    The idea that ants can change their foraging tactics according to changing resource abundance and distribution in order to optimise foraging efficiency or intake is not new (Carroll & Janzen 1973; Hlldobler & Lumsden 1980; Traniello 1989; Hlldobler & Wilson 1990; Nonacs & Dill 1990; Kay 2003; Dussutour & Simpson 2009). The abundance of a resource may often reveal little about its availability, i.e. the amount available to a consumer (Kay 2002). For example, red wood ants Formica truncorum consider an unpredictable protein resource qualitatively different from a stable sugar resource (Sundstrom 1993). In this experiment, two separate groups of foragers recruited to the different resources, while a reserve group of foragers contributed to higher recruitment rates at the two types of food. Of course, further experimentation would elucidate this possibility for A.gracilipes. But it seems likely that with such high numbers of foragers available, it would be optimal for ants to be taskpersistent for the type of resource they collect, saving time and energy on switching tasks.

    Management

    On Christmas Island, the dryseason preference for proteinbased food we have shown here has been exploited in an ongoing program of control for supercolonies of A.gracilipes. For more than a decade, a proteinbased toxic bait (Fipronil in fishmeal; Green & O'Dowd 2009, Boland etal. 2011) has been deployed during the dry season when workers typically show at least a 2:1 preference for proteinaceous food. This is reflected in the rapid recruitment of workers to and monopolisation of the bait within minutes during experimental trials (Marr etal. 2003).

    More generally, the eradication of invasive ants is notoriously difficult (Hoffmann & Saul 2010; but see Hoffmann & O'Connor 2004; Causton etal. 2005), so mitigation of their impacts usually relies on control programs that use toxic baits embedded in an attractive bait matrix. Timed correctly, the deployment of attractive toxic bait before the production of sexual brood would halt the potential increase in egglaying capabilities of a colony, eliminate workers that might escort queens to new nesting sites, and kill newly mated alate queens and prevent them from flying to found new nests. Knowledge of reproductive phenology and seasonal food preference is likely to play a pivotal role in designing effective control programs for invasive ants.
